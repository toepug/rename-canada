The asset is a six-month national argument, conducted in 1964, over what the country's flag should be. The Secretariat notes with some discomfort that the argument was genuinely bitter, genuinely national, and genuinely about something — three properties the Program's own consultations have been unable to reproduce — and that it nonetheless concluded, permanently, before the year was out.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 The question (June 1964)

In 1964, Canada had no flag of its own in law. It flew the Red Ensign — the British maritime flag with the Canadian coat of arms — and Prime Minister **Lester B. Pearson**, keeping an election pledge, opened the question of a distinctive national flag with the country's centennial approaching. What followed was one of the fiercest peacetime debates in parliamentary history. Opposition leader **John Diefenbaker** fought the change relentlessly, defending the Ensign and the heritage it carried; the government benches fought back; the argument consumed the House through the summer and autumn. It divided regions, generations, veterans' halls, and dinner tables. It was, in short, a country arguing seriously about what it meant — an activity the Secretariat notes is now handled by vendors.

### 2.2 The designs

The public sent in thousands of designs. Beavers featured heavily, as did maple leaves in every quantity and arrangement. Pearson's own preferred design — three red maple leaves between two blue bars, nicknamed the **"Pearson Pennant"** — became a lightning rod. A fifteen-member all-party committee was struck to sift the possibilities, and among them was a clean, stark design based on the Royal Military College flag, proposed by the historian **George Stanley**: red, white, red, with a single red maple leaf.

### 2.3 The vote (October 1964)

The committee's final vote produced one of the finest accidents in Canadian political history. Conservative members, expecting Liberal members to vote for the Prime Minister's pennant, voted for the Stanley design to deny Pearson his personal choice. The Liberal members voted for the Stanley design too. **The result was unanimous, 14–0, and neither side had entirely intended it.** The committee had selected, by mutual miscalculation, the better flag.

The Secretariat's methodology unit has reviewed this outcome and classified it as *consensus by error*. A proposal to formalize the technique was returned unread.

### 2.4 Resolution and service (December 1964–present)

After further weeks of filibuster, the government invoked closure; the House approved the flag 163 to 78 in the early hours of 15 December 1964, and on **15 February 1965** the maple leaf flag was raised for the first time on Parliament Hill. Many who had opposed it came, in time, to carry it. The stylized leaf was drawn with eleven points — a number chosen not for symbolism but for legibility, after testing showed a simpler leaf held its shape in wind. The eleven points mean nothing.

The flag has now been in service for 61 years.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Compliance findings

The asset fails the Program's core criterion in the manner typical of its class: it happened. It is further entangled with named persons (Pearson, Diefenbaker, Stanley), a specific date now marked annually as National Flag of Canada Day, and an output displayed on a substantial fraction of the nation's luggage.

The review panel records one partial finding in mitigation: **the eleven points of the maple leaf carry no meaning whatsoever.** This remains the only element of a major national symbol to achieve full compliance with the Program's criterion, and it did so in 1964, unassisted. The finding has been forwarded to Meridian Narrative Partners with a request for comment. A response is expected within the warranty period.

### 3.2 Precedent hazard (cross-referenced)

The asset presents the same hazard profile documented in the [Precedent File](/archive/1867-naming-exercise): a national-symbol question resolved quickly, cheaply, and permanently, under adversarial conditions the Program's engagement model classifies as unworkable. Resolution time — six months, including a filibuster — is non-compliant with current process standards, falling materially short of the Program's minimum viable duration. The file has accordingly been flagged for restricted internal circulation.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset scheduled; asset placed in the deferred queue pending resolution of the fact that the event's output is currently attached to flagpoles. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
