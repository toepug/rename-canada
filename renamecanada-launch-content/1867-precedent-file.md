## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 The candidate slate

As Confederation approached in the 1860s, alternative names for the proposed union circulated in the press and in Parliament. The attested slate included:

- **Tuponia** — from "The United Provinces of North America"; see [Rejected Designations, A.2](/archive/rejected-designations#a2-tuponia)
- **Borealia** — from *borealis*, "northern"; conceived as a counterpart to Australia, the southern land answered by a northern one
- **Hochelaga** — the St. Lawrence Iroquoian village at the foot of the mountain Cartier named Mont Royal in 1535, on the island where Montreal now stands; the proposal would have named the country for the second of the two villages Cartier recorded (for the first, see [Asset File 0001](/archive/canada))
- **Cabotia** — for John Cabot, who made landfall on the Atlantic coast in 1497 under English commission
- **Efisga** — an acronym: England, France, Ireland, Scotland, Germany, Aboriginal lands; an early and structurally doomed attempt to make a name by enumerating everyone
- **Laurentia** — see [Rejected Designations, A.3](/archive/rejected-designations#a3-laurentia)

The Secretariat notes that the 1860s produced, without process, a slate exhibiting the full taxonomy the Program later required eleven months and a Cross-Cultural Resonance Matrix™ to develop: the constructed word, the classical coinage, the Indigenous place name, the founder tribute, and the stakeholder acronym. This finding has been classified as coincidence.

### 2.2 The intervention

On 9 February 1865, during the Confederation debates in the Legislative Assembly, Thomas D'Arcy McGee — journalist, poet, Father of Confederation, and the finest orator of the era — rose and disposed of the entire slate in a single remark, inviting members to imagine waking one morning to find themselves no longer Canadians but Tuponians, or Hochelaganders. The chamber laughed. No candidate name recovered. "Canada," already three centuries old in the valley and carried by the people themselves, stood essentially unopposed thereafter.

The record thus shows total stakeholder alignment achieved by one uncredentialled humourist in under a minute, at no cost. The Program has been unable to replicate this result and has ceased attempting to (see 4.0).

McGee did not live to see much of the country he named by defending its name. He was assassinated in Ottawa in April 1868, ten months after Confederation — one of the very few political assassinations in Canadian history. His state funeral in Montreal drew crowds estimated at a scale the young country had never seen.

### 2.3 Kingdom, Dominion, and a psalm

The remaining question was not the name but the title. Several delegates favoured the **Kingdom of Canada**; British officials worried the word "Kingdom" would provoke republican sensibilities in Washington. At the London Conference of 1866–67, **Samuel Leonard Tilley** of New Brunswick proposed the solution from Psalm 72:8 — *"He shall have dominion also from sea to sea"* — and the **Dominion of Canada** entered the constitution, along with, eventually, the national motto (*A Mari Usque Ad Mare*).

The Secretariat's compliance unit has reviewed this episode and confirms that sourcing a national designation from scripture constitutes use of an unlicensed naming framework. The framework's output nonetheless entered service in 1867 and functioned as designed.

### 2.4 Subsequent modification (1982)

The precedent contains one further hazard. In July 1982, a private member's bill renaming **Dominion Day** to **Canada Day** passed the House of Commons in a matter of minutes, with barely a quorum present, and became law that fall. Whatever one's view of the change — and Canadians argued about it for years, which the Secretariat notes is itself a historical association — the operative fact is administrative: a successful national renaming was executed with no pilot program, no phases, no consultants, and a legislative cost approaching zero.

This file has been flagged RESTRICTED — MORALE.

## 3.0 Hazard assessment

The 1867 exercise presents four hazards to the current Program:

1. **Speed.** The exercise resolved the naming question faster than the Program's standard intake acknowledgment window (14–16 months).
2. **Cost.** A total naming expenditure of $0 establishes an anchoring figure that stakeholders may cite. Staff encountering this figure should refer inquiries to the Feasibility Study's discussion of modern signage complexity.
3. **Humour as methodology.** The decisive instrument was a joke. The Program's evaluation framework contains no field for this and cannot score it. A proposal to add one was rejected as non-compliant with dignity criteria.
4. **Durability of output.** The exercise's output has remained in continuous service for 159 years, materially exceeding the design life of any deliverable Meridian has agreed to warranty (18 months).

## 4.0 Mitigation

The Secretariat has determined that the 1867 precedent, while superficially successful, is non-replicable under modern conditions, primarily because it has already happened, which renders it historical, which renders it non-compliant. The precedent is accordingly out of scope, and the Program will proceed.

Phase 0 scoping has confirmed this conclusion and referred it for additional scoping.
