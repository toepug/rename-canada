The asset is a single apple tree, found growing wild in 1811, whose every commercial descendant — grafted, never grown from seed, for two centuries — is genetically identical to a tree that died in 1910. It shares its name, at one letter's distance, with a widely used personal computer. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A seedling in a clearing (1811)

**John McIntosh**, clearing overgrown land on his farm near Dundela, Upper Canada, found a stand of wild apple seedlings in 1811. One produced fruit of exceptional quality. He and his wife transplanted it near their home and began cultivating it.

### 2.2 Grafting, not breeding (1835–1900)

Apple trees do not reproduce true from seed; every seed produces a genetically distinct tree. The only way to reproduce the exact variety McIntosh had found was to graft cuttings from the original tree onto other rootstock, which the family began doing in 1835. Commercial-scale production began around 1870, and by 1900 the variety was common across Ontario, Quebec, and the northeastern United States.

### 2.3 The original tree (1894–1910)

A fire that destroyed the McIntosh family home in 1894 also badly damaged the original tree. McIntosh's son Allan nursed it for over a decade; it bore its last crop in 1908 and fell over in 1910, close to a century after its discovery. A monument erected at the site in 1912 gives the discovery date as 1796. The error has never been corrected.

### 2.4 A computer, a letter, and a lawsuit (1979–1986)

Apple employee Jef Raskin named his computer project after his favourite apple variety in the late 1970s. Concerned that the spelling "McIntosh" would create a trademark conflict with McIntosh Laboratory, a high-fidelity audio equipment manufacturer, he altered it to "Macintosh." Apple approached McIntosh Laboratory for permission to use the name in November 1982, licensed it in March 1983, and purchased the trademark outright in 1986. The settlement is commonly cited at $100,000, a figure McIntosh Laboratory's own counsel has called substantially understated. The exact sum has never been disclosed.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation presents a compliance profile without precedent in this Program's inventory: the originating specimen is deceased, has been for over a century, and left no seed-grown offspring whatsoever. Every one of its descendants, believed to number in the billions, is a graft, genetically identical to a tree that no longer exists. A separate and unrelated entity has, in addition, licensed a one-letter variant of the designation for commercial use since 1983. The Program's core criterion does not anticipate an asset that is simultaneously extinct, infinite, and partially owned by a computer company. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Custodial constraints (self-defeating finding)

The originating tree no longer exists; only a commemorative marker remains, and that marker has, since 1912, stated the wrong year. Every living instance of the asset is a genetic clone maintained by individual orchardists across multiple countries, none of whom hold any formal relationship with this Secretariat or with one another. There is no remaining original to serve notice on, and no single custodian empowered to receive notice on the asset's behalf.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, one graft at a time. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
