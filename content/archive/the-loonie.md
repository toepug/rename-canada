The asset is a national currency symbol that exists because the Royal Canadian Mint chose the cheaper of two shipping options in 1986. The dies for the coin's intended design have never been found. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A voyageur, fifty years in circulation (1935–1986)

**Emanuel Hahn**'s design for the 1935 silver dollar, struck to mark King George V's Silver Jubilee, showed a voyageur and an Indigenous guide paddling a birch-bark canoe, with faint lines in the background suggesting the Northern Lights. It remained in continuous circulation for over fifty years — a large silver coin from 1935 to 1968, a smaller nickel one from 1968 to 1986. When the government announced in 1986 that the paper one-dollar bill would be replaced by a smaller, gold-coloured coin, the plan was to carry the voyageur design forward onto the new format.

### 2.2 A courier and $43.50 (November 1986)

On 3 November 1986, the Royal Canadian Mint shipped the master dies for the new voyageur coin from its Ottawa facility to the production mint in Winnipeg, using a local courier service selected because it cost $43.50 less than the alternative. The dies never arrived. They have never been recovered.

### 2.3 Lost, or taken (1986–1987)

A House of Commons committee investigating the loss found that the Mint had no documented procedure for transporting master dies. The RCMP concluded the dies had simply been lost in transit; the Mint itself suspected theft, on the theory that the dies could be used to strike counterfeit coins. With the intended design compromised, officials turned to an existing design already on file: wildlife artist **Robert-Ralph Carmichael**'s rendering of a common loon, submitted to the Mint in 1978 and held in its bank of unused designs. New dies were cut, and the coin — bearing the loon rather than the voyageur — entered circulation on 30 June 1987, with 80 million coins distributed to banks. The dollar bill and the new coin circulated together for two more years; the final one-dollar bill was printed on 30 June 1989, exactly two years to the day after the loonie's introduction.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation's present form exists as a direct consequence of a $43.50 shipping decision. Standard Program practice assumes a designation's features were the product of deliberate selection; the Secretariat's review found instead that the asset's defining image was chosen because the Mint's first choice was lost in a courier's van, and no comparably prepared alternative was closer to hand. For reference, the Program's own candidate designation Norterra™ was developed for $340,000. The Loonie's defining redesign was completed for a documented savings of $43.50. The Secretariat has elected not to calculate the ratio.

### 3.2 Unresolved-investigation constraints (self-defeating finding)

The Mint's own inquiry into the dies' disappearance remains, by RCMP account, unresolved. Any compliance review of this designation's origin necessarily reopens a nearly forty-year-old case file the Mint itself was unable to close. The Secretariat has determined that reopening a stalled investigation exceeds its authority, its budget, and, per the minutes of the file's consultation, its interest.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a missing-persons file for two engraved steel dies. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
