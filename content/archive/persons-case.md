The asset is a five-year legal reference that changed the meaning of a single word in Canada's constitution, and the doctrine of interpretation the ruling used to do it. The Program's own core criterion requires that a winning designation carry no association with any historical event and, so far as practicable, no meaning capable of growing into one. This asset is the direct legal ancestor of a rule holding that meaning is allowed to grow. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Five women and a reference question (1927)

On 27 August 1927, **Emily Murphy** — an Edmonton magistrate who had herself been proposed for, and advised she was ineligible for, a Senate appointment — convened **Nellie McClung**, **Irene Parlby**, **Louise McKinney**, and **Henrietta Muir Edwards** at her home. The five petitioned the federal government to refer a question directly to the Supreme Court of Canada: whether the word "persons" in section 24 of the *British North America Act, 1867*, which set the qualifications for Senate appointment, included women.

### 2.2 "Not persons" (March–April 1928)

The Supreme Court heard the reference on 14 March 1928 and ruled unanimously, on 24 April 1928, that women were not "qualified persons" within the meaning of section 24 — reasoning that the word carried the meaning understood at the time the Act was written in 1867, when women could not vote or hold most forms of public office.

### 2.3 A living tree (18 October 1929)

The five appealed to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in London, then Canada's final court of appeal. On 18 October 1929, the Committee reversed the Supreme Court. Writing for the Committee, **Lord Sankey** held that the exclusion of women from public office was "a relic of days more barbarous than ours," and that the *British North America Act* had "planted in Canada a living tree capable of growth and expansion within its natural limits." The ruling established what Canadian courts have since called the **living tree doctrine**: the principle that Canada's constitution is to be read broadly and progressively, its meaning permitted to grow with the society it governs rather than fixed to the understanding of the men who wrote it.

### 2.4 Afterward (1930–present)

Cairine Wilson became the first woman appointed to the Senate of Canada on 15 February 1930, four months after the ruling. Statues of the five petitioners, titled *Women Are Persons!*, were unveiled in Calgary's Olympic Plaza on 18 October 1999 — the ruling's seventieth anniversary — and on Parliament Hill in Ottawa exactly one year later; the Ottawa monument was relocated beside the Senate of Canada Building in 2019 during Centre Block's rehabilitation. From 2004 to 2012, the five appeared on the Canadian fifty-dollar bill, the first identifiable women other than the reigning monarch on Canadian currency. Since 1979, the Governor General's Awards in Commemoration of the Persons Case have been presented each 18 October — observed nationally as **Persons Day** — to Canadians advancing equality for women and girls. The living tree doctrine itself remains the leading approach to constitutional interpretation used by Canadian courts today.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Doctrinal exposure

The designation is inseparable from a legal doctrine that is, on its own terms, unfriendly to this Program's founding premise. The core criterion requires that a winning designation carry no association with any historical event and, so far as reasonably practicable, no meaning capable of accruing one over time. The living tree doctrine holds that constitutional language is not fixed at the moment of its drafting but continues to acquire meaning as it is read. Legal counsel has advised that, under this doctrine, there is no principled basis on which any designation — including one deliberately engineered to mean nothing — can be guaranteed to go on meaning nothing indefinitely. Meridian was asked to comment and declined, citing scope.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional constraints (self-defeating finding)

The body that issued this ruling, the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council, ceased to hold any appellate jurisdiction over Canada in 1949 and cannot be petitioned to reconsider. The doctrine it established has since been relied upon by the Supreme Court of Canada in numerous subsequent constitutional rulings. Any action against this designation would accordingly require, as a precondition, the unwinding of nearly a century of constitutional jurisprudence built on top of it. No comparable authority is available to the Secretariat.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the doctrine that prevents its own review from ever concluding. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
