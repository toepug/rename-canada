The asset is a British Arctic expedition that departed in 1845 and was lost with all 129 hands. For more than a century, Inuit oral history recorded the location of both ships. In 2014 and 2016, guided substantially by that testimony, Parks Canada confirmed the wrecks of HMS Erebus and HMS Terror exactly where Inuit accounts had placed them. This finding, and the circumstances under which Inuit testimony was first dismissed, are discussed in Section 2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Departure and disappearance (1845–1848)

HMS Erebus and HMS Terror left Greenhithe, England, on 19 May 1845 under the command of Sir John Franklin, carrying 129 officers and men on a Royal Navy attempt to chart the last unmapped section of the Northwest Passage. Whalers sighted the ships in Baffin Bay that July. By September 1846 both were icebound in Victoria Strait, near King William Island, in what is now Nunavut. Franklin died on 11 June 1847. By the time the ships were abandoned on 22 April 1848, twenty-four men, including Franklin, had already died. The 105 who remained set out on foot toward the Back River. None survived. Forensic study of remains recovered on King William Island in the 1990s found cut marks consistent with the last survivors having resorted to cannibalism.

### 2.2 Inuit testimony and its dismissal (1854)

In 1854, while surveying the Boothia Peninsula for the Hudson's Bay Company, explorer **John Rae** met Inuit hunters and travellers, among them a man recorded as **In-nook-poo-zhee-jook**, who described tents, graves, and scattered human remains found on the mainland, and who possessed a Royal Navy officer's gold cap-band. Rae reported this testimony, along with recovered silver cutlery bearing officers' initials, to the Admiralty, including the Inuit account that some remains showed evidence of cannibalism.

The report reached London in 1854. Charles Dickens, writing in his journal *Household Words*, publicly rejected the Inuit testimony and went further, suggesting the Inuit themselves were responsible for the deaths. Lady Franklin campaigned against Rae for having credited Inuit sources at all. Rae claimed the £10,000 reward offered for information on the expedition's fate, but received none of the honours granted to other Arctic explorers of his era.

### 2.3 What was confirmed later (1990s–2016)

The forensic evidence described in 2.1 corroborated the Inuit accounts Rae had reported in 1854. Inuit oral tradition, meanwhile, continued to carry the location of the ships across generations of hunters and elders. In the 1990s, researcher David Woodman compiled and cross-referenced these accounts. Beginning in 2008, Inuit oral historian **Louie Kamookak** of Gjoa Haven, drawing on more than thirty years of interviews with elders, worked with Parks Canada archaeologist Robert Grenier to guide a renewed search. On 7 September 2014, a search team located a wreck in Wilmot and Crampton Bay; it was confirmed as HMS Erebus on 30 September 2014. That same day, in Westminster Abbey, a memorial to John Rae was unveiled — 160 years after his report was first dismissed. In 2016, a report from **Sammy Kogvik** of Gjoa Haven, who recalled seeing something protruding from sea ice years earlier, led searchers to HMS Terror within days, in the bay that already carried the ship's name.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from 129 named individuals, the events described in Section 2, and a body of Inuit oral tradition that the Program's classification criteria were not designed to evaluate. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Governance constraints (self-defeating finding)

The Wrecks of HMS Erebus and HMS Terror National Historic Site is administered under a 2023 Inuit Impact and Benefit Agreement between the Government of Canada, the Kitikmeot Inuit Association, and the Nattilik Heritage Society, with the Inuit Heritage Trust — the first national historic site in Canada structured to become, progressively, fully Inuit-led. This Secretariat holds no role in that agreement and no standing to alter it.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a governance agreement this Secretariat had no part in negotiating. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
