The asset is a ridge in northern France, captured by the four divisions of the Canadian Corps fighting together for the first time, over four days in April 1917, at a cost of 3,598 dead. The monument France granted Canada in perpetuity to mark it bears the names of 11,285 Canadians who died in France and have no known grave.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 The plan (October 1916 – April 1917)

Vimy Ridge, rising over the Douai Plain in northern France, had defeated previous French and British attempts to take it since 1914. Command of the assault fell to Lieutenant-General Sir Julian Byng, with the Canadian Corps' four divisions led by Major-Generals Arthur Currie, Henry Burstall, Louis Lipsett, and David Watson. Preparation ran nearly six months. Engineers dug twelve tunnel systems along a four-mile front, allowing troops to move to their assembly positions under cover. Soldiers rehearsed the attack on full-scale replicas of the German trench system built behind the lines, and 40,000 maps of the battlefield were distributed down to individual sections. Gunners registered and re-registered more than a million shells across roughly a thousand guns, refining a "creeping barrage" — a wall of artillery fire timed to advance just ahead of the infantry, one hundred yards every three to five minutes — that soldiers drilled to match on foot.

### 2.2 The four days (9–12 April 1917)

At 5:30 a.m. on Easter Monday, 9 April 1917, the barrage opened and roughly 15,000 Canadian soldiers went forward. Most of the ridge was taken that first day. The remainder, including the fortified height known as Hill 145, fell over the three days that followed. By nightfall on 12 April, the Canadian Corps held the ridge. The Corps had suffered 10,602 casualties. Of these, 3,598 men were killed.

### 2.3 The dead who have no grave (1917–1936)

Many of the Canadians who died in France during the war were never recovered, or were recovered without identification. In 1922, the government of France granted Canada perpetual use of just over 100 hectares of the ridge, including the ground fought over in April 1917, as a memorial site. Sculptor Walter Seymour Allward's design was built there between 1925 and 1936: twin limestone pylons rising above a walled base carved with figures representing sacrifice and mourning. Onto its walls are cut the names of 11,285 Canadian soldiers who died in France during the First World War and whose bodies were never identified.

### 2.4 The unveiling (26 July 1936)

More than 6,200 Canadian veterans and family members crossed the Atlantic for the memorial's unveiling — at the time, the largest peacetime movement of people from Canada to Europe. King Edward VIII unveiled the monument before a crowd estimated at over 100,000 on 26 July 1936.
