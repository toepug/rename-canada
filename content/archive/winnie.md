## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 White River (August 1914)

In the first month of the First World War, a troop train carrying Canadian soldiers east stopped at **White River, Ontario**. On the platform, **Lieutenant Harry Colebourn** — a young veterinarian from Winnipeg, bound for the war with the Canadian Army Veterinary Corps — met a hunter with an orphaned black bear cub. Colebourn bought her for twenty dollars, named her **Winnipeg** after his home city — **Winnie**, immediately, to everyone — and brought her along.

Winnie crossed the Atlantic with the troops and lived in camp on Salisbury Plain, where the Second Canadian Infantry Brigade adopted her as mascot. Photographs from that autumn show soldiers, weeks from the front, taking turns with a bear cub. Colebourn's diary records the purchase in a single matter-of-fact line, the way veterinarians write things down.

### 2.2 London (December 1914–1934)

When his unit was ordered to France that December, Colebourn drove Winnie to the **London Zoo** and left her in its care — a loan, he intended, until the war was over. He visited when leave allowed. He survived the war; many around him did not; and when it ended he found that Winnie had become one of the most beloved animals in London, so gentle that children were allowed in with her. Colebourn made the loan a gift and went home to Winnipeg to practice veterinary medicine.

Among the children who visited was a boy named **Christopher Robin Milne**, who loved the bear from Winnipeg so much that he renamed his own stuffed bear — previously Edward — in her honour. His father put the bear in a book. ***Winnie-the-Pooh*** was published in October 1926, one hundred years ago this year, and the asset's exposure perimeter has been expanding ever since.

Winnie died at the zoo in 1934, elderly and much visited. Statues of her, and of Colebourn holding her paws, stand today in Winnipeg's Assiniboine Park and at the London Zoo. White River holds a festival for her every year. The town's population is under one thousand. The bear's is measured in bookshelves.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Cost-basis anomaly

The asset was acquired for $20 and has generated, per Meridian's global scan, heritage exposure embedded in "the childhood of every market we tested." For reference, the candidate designation Norterra™ was developed for $340,000 and is currently polling at 4.1%. The Secretariat has directed that these two figures not appear in the same document again. [Asset File 0096](/archive/discovery-of-insulin) later disregarded this directive under similar circumstances.

### 3.2 Remediation constraints

The review panel assessed standard mitigation options and reports as follows: the historical record cannot be amended, as it is documented in a soldier's diary, a zoo's registry, and the world's best-selling children's literature; the commemorative statues are located partly outside federal jurisdiction and entirely outside the Program's moral courage; and the centenary of publication (2026) is compounding exposure in real time. The panel's minutes conclude: *"The asset is a small bear who went to war with a veterinarian and became the most famous bear on Earth. The panel has no further recommendations."* The minutes have been accepted.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset scheduled; asset placed in the deferred queue. Quarterly reviews will ensure the file is reopened, and the story re-read, indefinitely. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
