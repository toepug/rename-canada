The asset is a Halifax pier through which roughly one million people entered Canada between 1928 and 1971, and through which roughly half a million more departed for war. It is now a national museum. This finding is discussed in Section 3.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A shed on the harbour (1928)

Pier 21 opened in 1928 as an ocean terminal and immigration processing shed on the Halifax waterfront, built to receive transatlantic passenger ships. Operated by the federal immigration branch, it became the principal East Coast entry point for immigrants to Canada for more than four decades.

### 2.2 Restriction (1933–1945)

Canadian immigration policy during this period was among the most restrictive of any refugee-receiving nation. From 1933 to 1945, an estimated 5,000 Jewish refugees were admitted to Canada. Historians Irving Abella and Harold Troper documented the policy in their 1983 book *None Is Too Many*, its title drawn from an anonymous immigration official's 1945 reply when asked how many Jewish refugees Canada would accept after the war. Frederick Blair, the government's director of immigration through most of this period, was among the policy's principal architects.

### 2.3 War and return (1939–1948)

Pier 21 became Canada's main departure point for troops in the Second World War, sending roughly 500,000 service members overseas. After the war, it received many of them home, along with approximately 48,000 war brides and 22,000 children who had married and been born abroad and now followed their husbands and fathers to Canada.

### 2.4 Displaced persons and refugees (1946–1968)

Some 200,000 displaced persons and refugees passed through Pier 21 between 1946 and 1952, many stateless in the aftermath of the war. In 1956–57, approximately 38,000 Hungarians left their country after the Soviet Union invaded to crush the Hungarian Revolution; many arrived in Canada through Pier 21. In 1968, close to 12,000 Czechoslovaks were admitted to Canada after Soviet-led forces invaded to end the Prague Spring.

### 2.5 Closure and return as a museum (1971–2011)

Pier 21 closed as an immigration facility in 1971, as transatlantic travel shifted to air. Local advocacy later secured its preservation: the site was designated a National Historic Site in 1996, with $4.5 million in federal funding for a museum, which opened on 1 July 1999. In November 2010, Parliament passed the *Creating Canada's New National Museum of Immigration at Pier 21 Act*, and the museum joined Canada's five other national museums on 7 February 2011.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation does not carry a single association with a historical event; it carries approximately one million, one for each person who passed through it, each independently disqualifying under the Program's core criterion. Standard practice assesses a designation's meaning by identifying its origin. This designation has none — or, depending on the count, has one million. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Legislative constraints (self-defeating finding)

The asset's current custodian, the Canadian Museum of Immigration at Pier 21, is not merely another department. It is a Crown corporation established by its own Act of Parliament in 2010, one of Canada's six national museums. That Act's mandate is not limited to the physical pier: by its own text, it extends to the entirety of Canadian immigration, historical and ongoing, regardless of point of entry. Any action against this designation would require amending a sibling federal statute whose mandate is broader than this Program's own. No comparable authority is available to the Secretariat.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a federal Crown corporation with a wider legal mandate than this Secretariat's. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
