The asset is a two-year migration of roughly 100,000 people toward a creek in the Yukon that had, in a technical sense, already been claimed. Fewer than a third of them completed the journey. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A discovery already made twice (16 August 1896)

On 16 August 1896, **George Carmack**, along with **Keish** (known as Skookum Jim) and **Kaa Goox̱** (Tagish Charlie), found gold on Rabbit Creek, a tributary of the Klondike River later renamed Bonanza Creek, acting on a tip from prospector Robert Henderson. It has never been settled which of the three actually found it first. The group agreed that Carmack would be recorded as the official discoverer, out of concern that authorities would not recognize a claim registered to an Indigenous prospector. By 20 November 1896, 338 claims had been staked along the creek.

### 2.2 Tr'ochëk (August 1896 – 1900)

At the confluence of the Klondike and Yukon Rivers stood Tr'ochëk, a summer fishing camp used for generations by the Hän-speaking people who would become known as the Tr'ondëk Hwëch'in. Within a month of the Bonanza Creek discovery, thousands of stampeders arrived and occupied the site, pitching tents among the Tr'ondëk Hwëch'in's own dwellings. Chief Isaac negotiated a relocation with the Anglican Church and the North-West Mounted Police, and over the winter of 1897–98 the community began moving several kilometres downriver to a new village at Moosehide, a move completed by 1900.

The Tr'ondëk Hwëch'in First Nation is self-governing today under a 1998 land claim and self-government agreement. Tr'ochëk was designated a National Historic Site of Canada in 2002, at the First Nation's own request. Their history precedes this asset's record and is part of it.

### 2.3 The passes (1897–1898)

Stampeders reached the goldfields by one of two routes from the Alaska coast: the **Chilkoot Trail**, a steep route out of Dyea that Chilkoot Tlingit traders had used for centuries and initially controlled as paid packers, or the longer, gentler **White Pass** out of Skagway. At the summit of each, North-West Mounted Police posts enforced a requirement — prompted by a genuine shortage in Dawson the previous winter — that every person entering Canadian territory carry a full year's supplies, close to a ton of goods, before being allowed to proceed. On the Chilkoot's final ascent, workers cut some 1,500 steps into the ice, known as the Golden Stairs; stampeders climbed them single file, roped together, 50 to 60 pounds at a time, and moving one outfit over the pass could take up to 40 separate trips. The White Pass route earned a different name: the **Dead Horse Trail**, for the roughly 3,000 pack animals that died there from overwork and inexperienced handling.

### 2.4 Dawson City and the end of the rush (1898–1899)

North-West Mounted Police Superintendent **Sam Steele** arrived in Dawson on 24 June 1898 to oversee a town that had grown, in under two years, from nothing to a population of roughly 17,000 — briefly the largest Canadian settlement west of Winnipeg. The wider Klondike region held close to 30,000 people that summer. An estimated $29 million in gold was recovered between 1896 and 1899. The rush effectively ended the following summer, when gold was discovered near Nome, Alaska, and most of the stampeders who remained left for the new strike.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from a fixed discovery date, three named prospectors — one of them denied formal credit for reasons the Program's compliance unit is not presently equipped to revisit — a named North-West Mounted Police officer, and the occupation described in 2.2. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional constraints (self-defeating finding)

The rush's principal surviving infrastructure — the Chilkoot Trail and the Dawson Historical Complex — has, since 1998, formed part of the Klondike Gold Rush International Historical Park, a joint designation administered concurrently by Parks Canada and the United States National Park Service. Any deprecation action would accordingly require the concurrence of a foreign government, which lies outside any authority delegated to this Secretariat. The panel further notes that the asset in question ended itself in 1899, when a competing discovery in Alaska drew the stampeders away, more than a century before this or any Program existed to schedule it.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, pending a bilateral consultation this Secretariat has no standing to request. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
