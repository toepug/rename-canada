The asset is a fictional orphan placed, by a nineteenth-century publishing error of fortune rather than any decision of the Secretariat's, at the centre of Prince Edward Island's tourism economy. She has never existed and has generated more than a century of real economic activity, real diplomatic goodwill, and a real federally protected historic site on the strength of it. The review panel's standard procedure assumes an asset that happened. This one did not, and the finding this produces is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A manuscript in a hat box (1905–1908)

**Lucy Maud Montgomery**, raised by her grandparents in Cavendish, Prince Edward Island, after her mother's death in 1876, wrote the manuscript that became *Anne of Green Gables* in 1905. Several publishers rejected it. She put it away in a hat box and, by her own later account, nearly left it there. In 1907 she found it again, sent it out once more, and it was accepted by L. C. Page & Co. of Boston. The novel was published on **13 June 1908** and sold roughly 19,000 copies in its first five months. The farmhouse that inspired Green Gables belonged to Montgomery's cousins, the Macneill family, and stood a short walk from the grandparents' home where she was raised.

### 2.2 Avonlea becomes a destination

The fictional Avonlea was closely modelled on Cavendish, and readers began arriving to see it almost as soon as the books did. The farmhouse itself is now **Green Gables Heritage Place**, administered by Parks Canada as part of Prince Edward Island National Park under the Historic Sites and Monuments Act — a federal heritage site built, in effect, around the childhood home of a person who did not exist. Anne of Green Gables — The Musical has run most summers at Charlottetown's Confederation Centre of the Arts since 1965, and in 2014 was recognized by Guinness World Records as the longest continuously running annual musical theatre production on Earth; the production has since been staged more than 2,500 times internationally.

### 2.3 A global afterlife

In 1952, Japanese translator **Hanako Muraoka** — who had carried the English manuscript through wartime air raids after receiving it from a Canadian missionary friend in 1939 — published *Akage no An* ("Red-Haired Anne"), the first Japanese edition. It became a bestseller, entered the Japanese school curriculum in the 1970s, and has since appeared in more than thirty separate Japanese translations. Prince Edward Island has drawn Japanese visitors ever since; roughly one in five of the Island's tourists arrives from Japan, and Green Gables now hosts civil wedding ceremonies for couples — some Japanese, some not — married in front of the same fireplace where Montgomery herself once stood.

### 2.4 Ownership of a public-domain character

The novel entered the public domain in the United States in 1983 and in Canada in 1992 [VERIFY exact date]. This does not mean the character is unrestricted. The **Anne of Green Gables Licensing Authority Inc.**, jointly held by the Province of Prince Edward Island and the Heirs of L. M. Montgomery Inc., controls commercial use of Anne's name and likeness through **official marks** registered under section 9 of the Trade-marks Act — a category of mark that, unlike an ordinary trademark, does not expire. In 2023, a New York theatrical production company, Anne With An E, LLC, sued the Authority over its threatened trademark claims against their public-domain-based musical; both sides agreed to dismiss all claims that July.

Montgomery died in Toronto in 1942, at the age of 67.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Compliance findings (category ambiguity)

The Program's core criterion asks whether a designation carries any association with a historical event, figure, or tradition. The panel notes, with some evident discomfort, that Anne of Green Gables is not associated with a historical event because no such event occurred. Meridian's Fact Density instrument nonetheless registered a severe reading, on the basis that 118 years of continuous economic, touristic, and cross-national activity constitutes a fact in itself, whatever produced it. The asset is accordingly classified Tier One (Concerning) — Fictional.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional and custodial constraints (self-defeating finding)

The Secretariat's authority does not extend to either instrument presently governing this asset. The physical site associated with the character — Green Gables Heritage Place — is administered by Parks Canada under separate federal legislation. The character's name and image are separately controlled, in perpetuity, by the Anne of Green Gables Licensing Authority Inc., whose official marks do not lapse and which counsel has advised the Secretariat has no standing to contest, reissue, or bring under Program authority. No asset in the current inventory is governed by two authorities simultaneously, neither of which is this one.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the two jurisdictional instruments preventing any further action. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
