The asset marks the first confirmed reception of a wireless signal transmitted across the Atlantic Ocean, heard through a telephone earpiece over a wire antenna held aloft by a kite. The building in which it happened no longer exists. The jurisdiction in which it happened was not, at the time, Canada. Both findings are discussed in Section 3.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Poldhu to Signal Hill (December 1901)

**Guglielmo Marconi**, having already demonstrated wireless telegraphy over shorter distances in Europe, set out to prove that radio signals could cross an ocean. His transmitting station at **Poldhu, Cornwall**, on England's southwest coast, was the strongest he could build. For a receiving site he chose **Signal Hill**, a promontory overlooking the harbour at St. John's, Newfoundland, for its height and its unobstructed exposure to the open Atlantic. Marconi and his assistant, **George Kemp**, landed at St. John's on 6 December 1901 and set up their apparatus in an abandoned fever and diphtheria hospital standing on the hill. High winds destroyed two attempts to raise an antenna by balloon; a kite eventually held a wire aloft long enough to receive.

### 2.2 Three clicks (12 December 1901)

On the afternoon of 12 December, Marconi and Kemp heard the faint repeated clicks of Morse code for the letter **"S"** — three dots, transmitted from Poldhu roughly 3,440 kilometres away. Marconi's diary entry for the day records it without embellishment: *"Sigs. at 12:30, 1:10 and 2:20."* Eleven further signals were logged the following day; none were received on the Saturday after. Because the signal existed only as sound in a telephone earpiece — there was no means of producing an independent physical record — contemporaries, including rival telegraph interests with a financial stake in doubting him, questioned the claim for some time afterward. It is now accepted as accurate.

### 2.3 A monopoly's objection (December 1901 – 1902)

Within days of the announcement, the **Anglo-American Telegraph Company**, which held an exclusive charter over telegraph communications in Newfoundland and stood to lose business to any working wireless alternative, threatened Marconi with legal action if he continued operating on the island. Rather than contest the monopoly, Marconi left Newfoundland for Cape Breton, Nova Scotia, where local officials offered support instead of a lawsuit. He built a new station at Table Head, Glace Bay, and in December 1902 completed the first two-way transatlantic wireless exchange from Canadian — at that time, unambiguously Canadian — soil.

### 2.4 What remains

The original receiving building on Signal Hill is gone, destroyed by a later fire; nothing marks its exact footprint today. The structure now most associated with the achievement, **Cabot Tower**, was already under construction nearby when Marconi arrived — begun in 1897 for Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee and the 400th anniversary of John Cabot's 1497 landfall, and completed in 1900 — and was not connected to the December 1901 reception. It later housed an unrelated Marconi wireless station from 1933 to 1960. Newfoundland remained a separate British Dominion, then a British possession under direct rule, until it joined Canada as its tenth province on **31 March 1949**, forty-eight years after the events described above. Signal Hill is today a Parks Canada National Historic Site.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure (jurisdictional origin)

The asset fails the Program's core criterion on every available axis: a fixed date, a named inventor, a corporate dispute, and a monarch's jubilee all attach to it directly. Meridian's review further notes that the achievement's status as a Canadian one is itself the product of a later political act — Newfoundland's 1949 union with Canada — rather than anything true of the event when it happened. Fact Density was assessed as severe, "compounded by acquisition."

### 3.2 Physical and jurisdictional constraints (self-defeating finding)

The review panel's standard procedure could not be completed as designed. The building in which the reception occurred does not exist to be scheduled for anything; it burned down independently of this or any Program. The tower most visibly associated with the achievement today was not present for it and, in any case, sits within a Parks Canada National Historic Site outside the Secretariat's jurisdiction. No physical target and no available authority exist in the same place at the same time.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the finding that no comparable action was available to begin with. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
