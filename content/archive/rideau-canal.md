The asset is a 202-kilometre canal linking Ottawa to Kingston by way of 47 locks, built as a secure military supply route in case of a second war with the United States that never came. It has remained in continuous operation since 1832 and has been inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2007. Its construction is also the deadliest civil engineering undertaking presently in the Program's inventory. Both findings are discussed in Section 3.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A canal built for a war that didn't come (1826)

After the War of 1812, British military planners judged the St. Lawrence River supply line between Montreal and Kingston too exposed, running for long stretches within sight of American territory. **Lieutenant Colonel John By** of the Royal Engineers was sent to build an inland alternative, connecting the Ottawa River to Lake Ontario at Kingston via the Rideau and Cataraqui river systems. Work began in 1826 at the point where the new canal would meet the Ottawa River — the site that grew into the settlement of Bytown.

### 2.2 Slackwater engineering (1826–1832)

Rather than dig a continuous artificial channel, By's design raised water levels with a series of dams to drown the rapids along the route, linking sixteen lakes and two rivers through forty-seven locks. The largest structure, a dam at Jones Falls, stood 110 metres long and 18 metres high. Private contractors — among them John Redpath and Thomas McKay — hired thousands of Irish, Scottish, French-Canadian, and Algonquin labourers to carry out the work.

### 2.3 The cost in lives (1826–1832)

The labourers dug through swamp and bog at sites including the Isthmus near Kingston Mills, Chaffey's Mills, and Jones Falls, where standing water bred mosquitoes in large numbers. Malaria — known at the time as swamp fever — spread through the work camps, where conditions were crowded and sanitation poor, and shifts ran fourteen to sixteen hours a day, six days a week through the summer months. Estimates of the dead vary by source; a commonly cited figure holds that roughly 1,000 workers died over the six years of construction, about half of them from malaria [VERIFY exact malaria-specific count]. Most were Irish immigrants; some accounts of the toll include wives and children who had accompanied them to the camps.

A granite Celtic cross near Lock 1 in Ottawa, first raised in 2004 and rebuilt in 2018 after being destroyed by vandalism, commemorates them — by name where names survive, and by symbol where they do not: an Irish harp, a pick and shovel, a mosquito. Their record is part of this asset's record.

### 2.4 Completion and aftermath (1832)

The canal was completed in 1832 at a final cost of roughly £800,000, against an original estimate of £169,000. The overrun became a political scandal in London; the British Treasury recalled Colonel By in 1832 and accused him of unauthorized expenditure. A parliamentary committee later exonerated him, but he never received formal commendation for the work. He retired to Sussex and died on 1 February 1836. The settlement at the canal's northern terminus, named Bytown in his honour, was renamed Ottawa in 1855 and later became the national capital.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and cost exposure

The designation is inseparable from a named British officer, a documented cost-overrun scandal, and the deaths recorded in 2.3. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 International and operational constraints (self-defeating finding)

The asset carries two compounding obstacles the review panel could not resolve. First, the canal has been inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2007, placing it under an international convention to which Canada is a signatory — a designation the Secretariat has no delegated authority to alter or withdraw. Second, the canal remains in active recreational and navigational use, most visibly each winter, when a stretch through downtown Ottawa is maintained as a public skating rink long promoted as among the largest naturally frozen rinks in the world. No comparable action is available to the Secretariat on either count.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the international convention preventing any further action. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
