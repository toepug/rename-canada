The asset is the ceremonial completion, by hammer, of a 4,800-kilometre railway that fulfilled a promise made to British Columbia at Confederation, delivered fourteen years after that promise was made. Its British Columbia sections were built substantially by Chinese labourers, whose employment, wages, and losses are documented in 2.3. This finding is discussed further in Section 3.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A promise, a scandal, and a delay (1871–1881)

When British Columbia joined Confederation in 1871, one condition was a railway linking it to the rest of Canada within ten years. In 1872 the contract was awarded to a syndicate headed by shipping magnate Sir Hugh Allan, who had provided the governing Conservatives with $360,000 in political donations. The arrangement became public in April 1873 as the **Pacific Scandal**, and Prime Minister Sir John A. Macdonald resigned on 5 November 1873. A new syndicate was chartered to build the Canadian Pacific Railway in 1881, a decade after the original deadline had already passed.

### 2.2 The mountain sections (1880–1885)

The most difficult stretch of the line ran through the Fraser Canyon and the Rocky and Selkirk Mountains of British Columbia. Contractor **Andrew Onderdonk** was engaged to build roughly 341 kilometres of track through this terrain, work that required blasting tunnels directly into canyon walls with nitroglycerin.

### 2.3 The Chinese labourers (1881–1885)

Onderdonk recruited an estimated 6,500 workers directly from China and several thousand more from among Chinese communities already in California, contributing to a total of roughly 17,000 Chinese labourers employed on the British Columbia sections between 1881 and 1884. They were paid approximately one dollar a day, less than white labourers received for the same work, and were required — unlike white crews — to buy their own food and supplies from company stores at inflated prices. They were not paid during the three winter months when construction halted; average annual savings amounted to about $43. Chinese crews worked separately from white crews and were assigned the most dangerous tasks, including the nitroglycerin blasting. The exact number who died is not known and estimates vary considerably by source, from roughly 600 to figures several times higher [VERIFY: exact death toll remains disputed among historians]; Onderdonk himself estimated three deaths for every kilometre of track laid through the Fraser Canyon alone.

On 20 July 1885 — more than three months before the line was complete — the federal government imposed a fifty-dollar head tax on new Chinese immigrants to Canada, a fee its own royal commission estimated the average Chinese labourer, earning about $300 a year, would struggle to pay. In 2006, the Government of Canada formally apologized in the House of Commons for the head tax and the exclusion that followed it, and offered symbolic payments to surviving payers and their spouses.

Their labour is part of this asset's record.

### 2.4 Craigellachie (7 November 1885)

At 9:22 a.m. on 7 November 1885, CPR financier **Donald Smith** drove a ceremonial spike into the completed line at Craigellachie, British Columbia. His first swing bent the spike badly enough that it had to be replaced; his second succeeded. It was one of roughly 30 million iron spikes used to build the railway. The photograph taken at the moment became one of the most reproduced images in Canadian history.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from a named financier, a specific ceremony date, a documented political scandal, and the labour history recorded in 2.3, which remains the subject of a distinct and continuing federal commitment. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Redress conflict (self-defeating finding)

In 2006, the Government of Canada issued a formal apology in the House of Commons for the head tax described in 2.3 — an act whose stated purpose was remembrance, not erasure. The Secretariat's mandate and the 2006 redress commitment are not presently reconcilable: this Program exists to remove historical association, and that commitment exists to preserve it. No comparable action is available to the Secretariat pending resolution of this conflict, which has been referred to Phase 0 scoping.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a federal commitment this Secretariat did not make and cannot amend. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
