The asset was a universal exposition staged across two islands built for the purpose in the St. Lawrence River, mounted as the centrepiece of the nation's centennial year and visited more times over six months than the country then had residents. Its physical remnants today answer to a private residential co-operative, a federal environmental agency, and a municipal parks authority — three custodians, none of them the Secretariat. This finding is discussed in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A fair Moscow declined to hold (1962–1963)

The Bureau International des Expositions had originally awarded the 1967 world's fair to Moscow. Late in 1962, the Soviet Union withdrew its bid, citing cost. Montreal mayor **Jean Drapeau** made a fresh presentation to the Bureau, and the fair was awarded to Canada instead. **Pierre Dupuy**, a career diplomat who had served as ambassador to France, Italy, and the Netherlands, was appointed Commissioner General in 1963 and spent the following years visiting more than 125 countries to secure participation.

### 2.2 Two islands built to hold it (1963–1967)

The site did not exist yet. Île Sainte-Hélène, a park in the St. Lawrence linked to Montreal by the Jacques Cartier Bridge, was expanded, and an entirely new island, Île Notre-Dame, was built beside it — roughly 25 million tons of rock and fill, ten to twelve percent of it excavated concurrently from the tunnels of the new Montreal Metro, then also under construction for the same centennial deadline.

### 2.3 Six months of the future (April–October 1967)

The fair's theme, "Terre des Hommes" — "Man and His World" — was chosen at a 1963 gathering of writers, artists, and politicians at Montebello, including novelist Gabrielle Roy, drawn from the title of Antoine de Saint-Exupéry's 1939 book. Sixty-two nations built pavilions. The fair drew 54,991,806 visitors over its run, including a single-day record of 569,500, against a national population of roughly 20 million — making Expo 67 one of the most attended and, by most retrospective accounts, most successful world's fairs of the twentieth century. Its two most enduring structures were **Habitat 67**, a modular housing complex designed by architect Moshe Safdie and developed from his McGill master's thesis, and the American Pavilion, a 76-metre geodesic dome designed by Buckminster Fuller.

### 2.4 Afterward

Habitat 67's units were sold to private owners after the fair and remain occupied residential housing today; the building was designated a federal heritage building in 2009, a recognition of architectural significance that carries no operating authority. The fairgrounds continued as a seasonal exhibition, also called "Man and His World," each summer from 1968 until 1981, when it closed. On 20 May 1976, a fire caused by welding work during renovations burned away the entire acrylic skin of the geodesic dome in about half an hour; no one was killed. The dome's steel frame stood empty for over a decade until the federal government acquired it in 1990, reopening it in 1995 as the Biosphère, an environmental museum. The surrounding islands, renamed Parc Jean-Drapeau in 1999 following the mayor's death that year, are today a municipal park under the City of Montreal.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is anchored to Canada's 1867 Confederation centennial, a named mayor, a named diplomat, a foreign superpower's Cold War-era withdrawal, and a line from French literature. The centennial link additionally connects this file directly to a designation the Program has already found non-compliant elsewhere (see 4.0). Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional fragmentation (self-defeating finding)

The review panel's standard procedure assumes a single site under a single authority. None remains here. Habitat 67 is privately owned residential property, federally recognized only as a heritage building with no operating jurisdiction attached. The Biosphère answers to Environment Canada. The surrounding grounds answer to the City of Montreal. The seasonal exhibition that once held these three sites together under one administration, "Man and His World," ceased operations after the 1981 season. No comparable single authority has existed since, and none is available to the Secretariat now.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the three unrelated authorities that would each need to agree to it first. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
