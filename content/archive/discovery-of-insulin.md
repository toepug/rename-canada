## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A pancreas and a summer away (1920–1921)

**Frederick Banting**, a surgeon with an idea about ligating pancreatic ducts to isolate the gland's internal secretion, approached **John Macleod**, professor of physiology at the University of Toronto, for laboratory space in the spring of 1921. Macleod assigned Banting a research assistant, medical student **Charles Best**, provided a small number of dogs for experimentation, and then left for a summer holiday in Scotland. Banting and Best carried out the initial experiments that summer largely on their own, returning promising results by the time Macleod came back in the autumn.

### 2.2 Impure to pure (autumn 1921 – January 1922)

The early pancreatic extracts were effective but impure enough to cause severe reactions in patients. Biochemist **James Collip**, from the University of Alberta and on sabbatical in Toronto, joined the team that autumn to refine the extraction and purification process. On 11 January 1922, **Leonard Thompson**, a fourteen-year-old boy dying of diabetes at Toronto General Hospital, received the first injection of the Banting–Best extract; the result was only partial, and the injection site abscessed. On 23 January 1922, Thompson received an injection of Collip's purified extract. His blood sugar returned to normal within a day.

### 2.3 A patent for one dollar (1922)

Concerned that a competing patent claim could interfere with production, or place the extraction process under a single company's control, Banting, Best, and Collip transferred their patent rights to the University of Toronto for one dollar each, an agreement finalized in December 1922 and formally recorded on 1 January 1923. The university, in turn, licensed the process broadly and without exclusivity, including to Connaught Laboratories in Toronto and Eli Lilly and Company in the United States, to scale production as quickly as possible. A quotation attributed to Banting — "insulin does not belong to me, it belongs to the world" — is widely repeated but does not appear in any of his own writings; its earliest known source is a children's biography published in 1959, eighteen years after his death.

### 2.4 A prize for two (1923)

The 1923 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded to Banting and Macleod, making them the first Canadians to receive a Nobel Prize in any category. Banting, then 32, remains the youngest laureate in the prize's history. Banting was reportedly furious that Best had been excluded from the award and split his half of the prize money with him. Macleod, in turn, split his half with Collip.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Valuation exposure

The Program's standard compensation methodology requires an established market valuation before a deprecation cost estimate can be produced. This asset's own discoverers fixed its value, by deliberate choice, at one dollar. For reference, the Program's own candidate designation Norterra™ was developed for $340,000. This is the second time this fiscal year a legacy asset's acquisition cost has been placed in the same document as that figure — the first was Asset File 1877's $20 — and the second time an instruction has been issued that a Norterra™ development cost not appear alongside a legacy asset's valuation again. The instruction has again not been followed.

### 3.2 Advisory non-engagement (self-defeating finding)

Meridian Narrative Partners' own review of this asset (*Legacy Risk Register*, s. 3.3) declined to complete a standard assessment, reporting that the file "resists reframing" and recommending it be scheduled last, in the furthest available phase, by someone else. The Secretariat accordingly has no compliant vendor deliverable on which to act.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, pending a Meridian deliverable that has been declined once already. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
