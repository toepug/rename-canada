The asset is a worldwide system for organizing time into twenty-four standard zones, proposed by a Canadian engineer after he missed a train, and adopted internationally in 1884. Every date, deadline, and quarterly review cycle referenced elsewhere in this Program's documentation, including this file's own, depends on the asset under review. This finding is discussed in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A missed train (July 1876)

**Sandford Fleming**, already a distinguished railway engineer, arrived at the station in Bandoran, County Donegal, Ireland, to find his train gone. A printed timetable had listed the departure as 5:35 p.m.; it left at 5:35 a.m. The error cost him a full day. Fleming concluded that a single, unambiguous, worldwide system of timekeeping would have made the mistake impossible.

### 2.2 A proposal (1879)

Fleming presented his proposal for worldwide standard time — twenty-four zones, each one hour apart, measured from an agreed prime meridian — to the Royal Canadian Institute in Toronto on 8 February 1879. His papers were judged significant enough that the British government forwarded copies to eighteen foreign governments that same year.

### 2.3 Washington (October 1884)

The International Meridian Conference convened in Washington, D.C., with delegates from more than twenty countries. It adopted Greenwich, England, as the prime meridian and a universal day beginning at Greenwich midnight. The conference's final resolutions differed in some particulars from Fleming's own proposal, but the system of standardized global time zones it enabled was substantially his design. Full adoption by all major countries was not complete until 1929, forty-five years later.

### 2.4 A stamp, a knighthood (1851–1915)

Decades before his work on time zones, Fleming designed the Three Penny Beaver, issued in 1851 as [Canada's first postage stamp](/archive/the-beaver) and the first anywhere to depict an animal rather than a monarch's portrait. He served as chief engineer of the Intercolonial Railway and surveyed much of the Canadian Pacific Railway's route through the Rocky Mountains. He was knighted in 1897 and served as chancellor of Queen's University from 1880 until his death, in Halifax, on 22 July 1915.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from a named engineer, a specific missed train, and a specific 1884 conference. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Recursive scheduling constraint (self-defeating finding)

This Program's quarterly review cycle, every date recorded in this file's Recent Updates table, and the concept of a Phase 0 preceding a Phase 1, all presuppose the existence of standardized, zone-based, universally agreed time — the asset presently under review. Any order scheduling this asset's deprecation would itself need to specify a date and time, expressed in a time zone, to take effect. No such date can be fixed without relying on the asset the order is meant to remove. The deprecation schedule depends on the asset.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed, on any date, for the reasons given in 3.2. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, using the asset. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
