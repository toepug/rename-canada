The asset is a semi-aquatic rodent, formally recognized as a symbol of Canadian sovereignty in 1975, and, for nearly two centuries before that recognition, the principal commodity of the trade network that mapped much of the country's interior. It is also alive, wild, and presently breeding in every province and territory without reference to this or any Program. This finding is discussed in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A commodity before a symbol (1670–1821)

In 1670, King Charles II granted the Hudson's Bay Company a royal charter over Rupert's Land — the entire drainage basin of Hudson Bay, covering roughly forty percent of the land mass of what later became Canada — for the "sole trade and commerce" of the territory. Beaver pelts, driven by European demand for felt hats, became the trade's principal commodity. The company's basic unit of account for its first two centuries was the **Made Beaver**: one prime beaver pelt, against which the value of imported goods such as tools, textiles, and firearms was set. Montreal-based merchants and Scottish traders founded a rival, the North West Company, in 1779; competition between the two companies was fierce until they merged in 1821 under the Hudson's Bay Company's name and charter.

### 2.2 A trade that reshaped economies (1670–1850)

Indigenous nations across the continent had already developed extensive trade networks by the time European traders arrived. The fur trade drew these networks into a new commercial system, in which Indigenous trappers, guides, and provisioners became essential to its operation. It also introduced new dependencies on European manufactured goods, altered political and economic relationships between nations, and began to reshape communal land use into claimable trapping territories. Economic historian Harold Innis credited the fur trade with linking the country's separate regions into a single economic system; the trade's routes and posts went on to form the basis of much of the transportation and administrative geography that followed. This history is well documented and is not treated further here as a matter of designation exposure.

### 2.3 Decline and shift (1821–1850s)

Sustained overharvesting sharply reduced beaver populations across much of the continent by the early nineteenth century. European fashion then shifted decisively from beaver-felt hats to silk hats through the 1840s, and beaver pelt prices collapsed within a few years from six to eight dollars per pound to about one dollar. By 1850, the fur trade that had organized much of the continent's colonial economy for a century and a half was a fraction of its former size.

### 2.4 From stamp to statute (1851–1975)

In 1851, the colonial Province of Canada issued the Three Penny Beaver, designed by Sandford Fleming (later knighted, in 1897) — the first postage stamp anywhere to feature an animal rather than a monarch's portrait. The beaver appeared on the Canadian five-cent coin beginning in 1937, in a design by G. E. Kruger-Gray. Official recognition came in 1975, and by way of an unusual trigger: in January of that year, a New York state senator introduced a bill to make the beaver that state's official animal. Within weeks, 23-year-old Member of Parliament Sean O'Sullivan drafted a one-sentence private member's bill — "An Act to provide for the recognition of the Beaver (*Castor canadensis*) as a symbol of the sovereignty of Canada" — which received royal assent on 24 March 1975.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation's own legislative record consists of a single sentence, adopted to preempt a competing claim from a neighbouring jurisdiction, a fact the Secretariat notes without further comment on its own $340,000-per-candidate development process. The designation is additionally inseparable from a nearly two-century trade monopoly and the economic reshaping documented in 2.2. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Operational constraints (self-defeating finding)

Standard Program procedure assumes an asset capable of being scheduled, contacted, or at minimum located. The asset is a wild, semi-aquatic mammal, distributed across every province and territory, and does not respond to consultation invitations, written or otherwise. Field researchers report the asset's population to be stable or increasing, and note multiple instances of individual specimens constructing unauthorized water-control infrastructure on federal and provincial land without any permitting process. No comparable action is available to the Secretariat.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed, nor, absent a change in the asset's reproductive habits, is it expected to become executable. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
