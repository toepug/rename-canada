The asset is a sterling silver bowl, originally purchased for ten guineas, awarded annually since 1893 to the champion of organized ice hockey in Canada and, since the 1920s, the National Hockey League. It is the only trophy in major professional sport on which the name of every member of the winning team — players, coaches, and staff — is individually engraved. The Secretariat's review found that the asset's legal ownership does not rest with any league, team, or government, complicating identification of a party against which a deprecation order could be addressed. This finding is discussed further in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A Governor General's gift (1892)

**Frederick Arthur Stanley, 16th Earl of Derby**, serving as Governor General of Canada, was persuaded by his sons and their Rideau Hall teammates to donate a challenge trophy for the country's leading hockey club. His aide, Captain Charles Colville, purchased a silver bowl for ten guineas from the London silversmiths G.R. Collis & Co. of Regent Street. Stanley announced the gift by letter on **18 March 1892**, read aloud at a banquet in Ottawa's Russell House Hotel honouring the Ottawa Hockey Club: a challenge cup, he wrote, to be held from year to year by the Dominion's champion team. It was registered as the **Dominion Hockey Challenge Cup**. Stanley himself never saw a game played for it — he returned to England later that year on the death of his brother and never came back to Canada.

### 2.2 A challenge cup among many claimants (1893–1926)

The first Cup was awarded in 1893 to the Montreal Hockey Club. For roughly three decades afterward, the trophy operated as its name promised: a standing champion could be challenged by another eligible club at any point in the season, and the Cup changed hands accordingly, travelling between senior amateur and, later, early professional leagues across the country. This arrangement ended by attrition rather than decree. In 1926, the Western Hockey League folded; the National Hockey League absorbed most of its players and, with no comparably resourced league left to mount a challenge, became the Cup's only realistic competitor for the championship in all but name.

### 2.3 Trust, not title (1947)

Stanley's original terms, set down with the trophy's donation, specified that the Cup would never become the property of any team, however many times it was won, and vested authority over disputes in two trustees appointed to administer it — not in any league, and not in the Crown. In 1947, the NHL and the trust's two surviving trustees, P. D. Ross and Cooper Smeaton, signed an agreement delegating administration of the competition — eligibility, format, scheduling — to the League. Ownership of the Cup itself was not part of what was delegated. It remains, to the present day, the property of an independent perpetual trust whose terms trace to 1892 and which the NHL does not control.

### 2.4 A record that keeps its mistakes

Three physical Stanley Cups presently exist. The original 1892 bowl was retired from active presentation in 1970, its silver grown too thin for further engraving, and is now displayed in the Vault Room of the Hockey Hall of Fame in Toronto. A **Presentation Cup**, an authorized duplicate made in 1963, is the trophy actually awarded each June and toured by champions for the following year. A third, the **Permanent Cup**, was produced in 1993 as a stand-in for display whenever the Presentation Cup is on the road — and, in the course of its production, had a number of the Presentation Cup's known engraving errors quietly corrected.

The engraving tradition itself — every winning player's name, cut into the silver, permanently — has produced close to a dozen documented mistakes over the trophy's history [VERIFY exact count], none of them ever corrected on the Cup that actually travels. Goaltender Jacques Plante's name is spelled a different way for each of his five championships. The 1962 Toronto Maple Leafs appear as the "Leaes" [VERIFY exact year]. The 1972 Boston Bruins are rendered "BQSTQN." Colorado's Adam Deadmarsh became "Deadmarch" in 1996. In 1907, the champion Montreal Wanderers left the Cup at a photography studio; a cleaner, not recognizing it, took it home and used it as a flowerpot for two months before anyone noticed it missing. A frequently repeated story holds that in 1905, an Ottawa player drop-kicked the Cup into the frozen Rideau Canal after a championship banquet; Ottawa Journal sports editor Bill Westwick and NHL president Frank Calder both later denied it happened, and the record is accordingly treated here as disputed rather than confirmed.

Since the 1980s, an official from the Hockey Hall of Fame — one of a small rotating group known informally as Keepers of the Cup — accompanies the trophy everywhere it travels, white gloves included.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from a single named individual — a British peer serving as Governor General — a specific date of donation, and a specific city of origin, each independently disqualifying under the Program's core criterion. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe. The asset additionally carries, by extension, the full engraved record described in 2.4: a physical object listing thousands of individual names accumulated over more than a century.

### 3.2 Ownership and jurisdiction (self-defeating finding)

Standard Program procedure presumes an identifiable owner against whom a deprecation order can be filed. Investigation found none available. The Cup is not the property of the National Hockey League, of any team, or of any government; it is held in a perpetual charitable trust established by the donor's own 1892 terms, administered by trustees whose authority the NHL was permitted, in 1947, only to *borrow* for competition purposes. The Secretariat notes further that one of the asset's three physical instances — the Permanent Cup — already undertook a form of historical correction in 1993, on its own initiative, without reference to this or any Program. No comparable authority is available to the Secretariat, and none is required to note the precedent.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the trust documents establishing why it cannot be reached. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
