The asset is an eight-game hockey series between Canada and the Soviet Union. Half its games, including the one in which it was decided, were played in Moscow, in an arena demolished in 2023, in a jurisdiction outside Canada's reach. This finding is discussed in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Professionals meet the Soviets (1972)

Under an agreement between Hockey Canada and Soviet hockey authorities, Canadian professional players faced the Soviet national team for the first time: an eight-game series, four games in Canada, four in Moscow. Team Canada, assembled from the country's best NHL players, was widely expected to win easily.

### 2.2 A country humbled, then rallied (2–22 September 1972)

The Soviet Union won the series opener in Montreal 7–3. Team Canada struggled through the four Canadian games, and after a loss in Vancouver in Game 4, the home crowd booed its own team off the ice. Phil Esposito, interviewed on national television immediately afterward, appealed directly to Canadians watching, visibly frustrated by the reception. The series moved to Moscow with Canada needing to win most of the remaining games to take the series.

### 2.3 Thirty-four seconds (28 September 1972)

Game 8 was played at the Luzhniki Palace of Sports in Moscow before a capacity crowd that included Soviet leadership. Canada trailed 5–3 after two periods. Goals by Phil Esposito and Yvan Cournoyer tied the game at 5–5 in the third. With 34 seconds remaining, **Paul Henderson** scored the series-deciding goal past goaltender Vladislav Tretiak. Broadcaster Foster Hewitt's call — "Henderson has scored for Canada!" — is among the most repeated sentences in Canadian broadcasting history. Canada won the game 6–5 and the series 4–3–1.

### 2.4 A country that stopped (1972–present)

An estimated 15 of Canada's 20 million people watched Game 8, many on televisions moved into schools and workplaces for the broadcast, carried by satellite. In 2012, the Government of Canada designated the Summit Series a National Historic Event. Commemorative plaques have since been unveiled in each of the four Canadian host cities, beginning with Montreal in 2016. The Luzhniki Palace of Sports, where the series was decided, was demolished in 2023.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from a named goal-scorer, a named broadcaster, a specific date, and a foreign nation, each independently disqualifying. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional constraints (self-defeating finding)

Four of the asset's eight defining events took place outside Canada, in the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics, a state that ceased to exist in 1991. The site of the series-deciding event, the Luzhniki Palace of Sports in Moscow, was demolished in 2023. The Secretariat accordingly has no jurisdiction over the location of the asset's central moment, no original state party remaining to consult, and, since 2023, no structure standing on which a plaque could be placed regardless. No comparable action is available.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a jurisdictional gap this Secretariat has no means of closing. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
