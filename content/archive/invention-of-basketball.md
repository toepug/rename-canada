The sport is now played under a single governing body in more than two hundred countries. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A problem in Springfield (December 1891)

**James Naismith**, a 30-year-old physical education instructor at the International YMCA Training School in Springfield, Massachusetts, was asked by his supervisor, Dr. Luther Gulick, to devise an indoor game that would keep students active between the football and baseball seasons. Naismith asked a janitor for two boxes; the janitor returned with two peach baskets instead. Naismith nailed them to the railing of the gymnasium's elevated running track, wrote thirteen rules, and had his class of eighteen play the first game — nine a side, with a soccer ball — on 21 December 1891.

### 2.2 A game with no patent (1891–1939)

Naismith never patented the game and drew no royalties from its spread. He went on to earn a medical degree in 1898 and later became athletic director and basketball coach at the University of Kansas.

### 2.3 A Scottish childhood, a Canadian birth, no Canadian citizenship (1861–1925)

Naismith was born near Almonte, in what is now Ontario, to a family of Scottish immigrants. As a child, he learned "duck-on-the-rock," a street game his father had played in Glasgow, which Naismith later credited as an influence on basketball's central design idea — an arcing shot rather than a thrown or driven one, intended to reduce roughness. Canadian citizenship did not exist as a formal legal status until the *Canadian Citizenship Act* took effect in 1947, eight years after Naismith's death in 1939; for his entire life, he was legally a British subject. He was naturalized as a United States citizen on 4 May 1925. A 2025 study by a University of Stirling researcher argued that Naismith's Scottish upbringing, accent, and self-identification merit recognition alongside his Canadian birthplace and American career.

### 2.4 An Olympic debut, crowdfunded (1936)

Basketball appeared as an Olympic demonstration sport in 1904 and became an official event at the 1936 Berlin Olympics. Naismith, then 74, attended only because his former student Forrest "Phog" Allen and the National Association of Basketball Coaches raised the funds for his travel. Naismith personally presented the medals — gold to the United States, silver to Canada, bronze to Mexico — and was named honorary president of the newly formed International Basketball Federation.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation presents an unusual compliance profile: the inventor was born in Ontario but held no Canadian citizenship, since none existed until 1947; the invention itself took place on American soil, in American employment, using improvised American equipment; and a 2025 academic paper has since argued the inventor's formative influences were substantially Scottish. The Program's standard provenance framework assumes a single, stable country of origin. This designation has three, none of which is dispositive. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional constraints (self-defeating finding)

The asset's flagship institution, the Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame, is located in Springfield, Massachusetts, on the site of the original invention. The sport's global governing body, FIBA, is headquartered in Mies, Switzerland, in a building that itself contains a room named for the inventor. No comparable institution exists on Canadian soil. The Secretariat notes, without further comment, that this appears to be the first asset in the current inventory for which no domestic custodian could be identified at all.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits, cannot presently be executed, and — for the first time in the Program's history — cannot presently be attempted, there being no domestic instrument to attempt it with. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, pending the discovery of a jurisdiction in which to begin. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
