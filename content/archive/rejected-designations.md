# Rejected Designations Annex

**From:** National Designation Renewal Secretariat

This annex records candidate designations reviewed by the Secretariat and rejected for non-compliance with the Program's core evaluation criterion: a compliant national identifier must carry **no association with any historical event whatsoever**.

The designations below were, without exception, rejected for having histories. The Secretariat is required under TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b) to document those histories in full before closing each file. It regrets the length of this page.

## A.1 Kanata

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| File | RD-2025-001 |
| Intake source | Public submissions (received 3,112 times — the most-submitted designation in Phase 0) |
| Etymology | Confirmed — **flagged** |
| Historical associations | Foundational |
| Status | **REJECTED — excessively rooted in heritage** |

### The record

*Kanata* is the St. Lawrence Iroquoian word meaning "village" or "settlement," recorded by Jacques Cartier at Stadacona in 1535 and the direct source of the name "Canada" (see [Asset File 0001](/archive/canada), s. 2.1). Adopting it would restore the national identifier to its original form — the word as it was actually spoken on the river, before four centuries of transliteration.

The word is not a relic. Though the St. Lawrence Iroquoian language itself is no longer spoken, its close relatives are: in Mohawk, a living Iroquoian language, *kaná:ta'* still means "town." A speaker in Kahnawà:ke or Akwesasne today uses, in ordinary conversation, a recognizable form of the word the country is named for. The proposal, in effect, asks the Program to adopt a living form of the word the country is already named for.

The name is also already in service. In the 1960s, the planned community of **Kanata, Ontario** was founded northwest of Ottawa, its name chosen deliberately to honour the word's origin. It grew into a city of tens of thousands and was amalgamated into Ottawa in 2001, where it remains — meaning the national capital already contains the proposed designation, functioning without incident, twenty minutes from Parliament Hill.

### Grounds for rejection

The review panel found the designation non-compliant on the following grounds:

1. The designation carries a direct, documented association with a specific historical event (the naming of Canada), a specific place (Stadacona), specific persons (Donnacona, Domagaya, Taignoagny), and a specific living linguistic tradition. This constitutes total heritage exposure.
2. The designation's meaning is verifiable. Verifiable meaning is incompatible with the Program's meaning-agnostic identity framework (cf. Meridian Narrative Partners, *Final Naming Architecture Report*, s. 4: "unlocking meaning-agnostic brand equity").
3. Adoption would imply that the answer to the naming question predates the Program, raising unresolvable questions regarding the Program.

**Disposition:** File closed. The 3,112 submissions were individually rejected for the same reason, per procedure. Submitters were notified within the standard 14–16 month window.

## A.2 Tuponia

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| File | RD-2025-002 |
| Intake source | Legacy process (1867); resubmitted through public intake, 2025 |
| Etymology | Constructed acronym |
| Status | **REJECTED — legacy process output** |

### The record

*Tuponia* — derived from "The United Provinces of North America" — was among the names seriously floated during the Confederation debates of the 1860s, alongside Borealia, Hochelaga, Cabotia, Efisga, and Laurentia. Thomas D'Arcy McGee disposed of the whole slate in Parliament in a single line, asking members to imagine waking "some fine morning and finding ourselves, instead of Canadians, Tuponians or Hochelegandians." The chamber laughed; Canada won. It is, on the record, one of the most efficient naming decisions in the country's history.

### Grounds for rejection

The designation was previously considered under the 1867 legacy exercise, a methodology the Secretariat has classified as non-compliant (no consultants engaged; see [Precedent File: The 1867 Naming Exercise](/archive/1867-naming-exercise)). Re-evaluating an output of that process would imply the process produced reusable outputs, a finding the Secretariat is not prepared to make. Additionally, the designation is now inseparable from the historical event of its own rejection, compounding exposure.

**Disposition:** File closed. Rejected twice, 158 years apart — a record.

## A.3 Laurentia

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| File | RD-2025-003 |
| Intake source | Legacy process (1867); independent public resubmission, 2025 |
| Etymology | From the St. Lawrence River |
| Historical associations | Approximately 1.5 billion years |
| Status | **REJECTED — temporally regressive beyond acceptable thresholds** |

### The record

*Laurentia* traces to the St. Lawrence River — itself named by Cartier, who entered the gulf around the feast day of St. Lawrence in 1535 — and to the Laurentian Mountains that rise north of it, among the oldest mountain ranges on Earth. Geologists later gave the name *Laurentia* to something older still: the ancient continental craton that forms the structural core of North America, assembled over 1.5 billion years ago. Every other name in this annex refers to history. This one refers to the rock the history happened on.

### Grounds for rejection

The review panel initially scored the designation favourably on distinctiveness before the etymology review disclosed the association with a geological formation predating the Secretariat by approximately 1.5 billion years. Program thresholds classify any association older than the Program itself as temporally regressive. This designation exceeds the threshold by the widest margin yet recorded and has been retained in the file as a calibration benchmark.

**Disposition:** File closed. The rock was not consulted and has filed no objection.

## A.4 Vinland

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| File | RD-2025-004 |
| Intake source | Public submission, 2025 |
| Etymology | Old Norse |
| Historical associations | Circa 1000 CE |
| Status | **REJECTED — pre-Confederation liability** |

### The record

*Vinland* is the name the Norse gave to the lands they reached at the western end of their Atlantic voyages around the year 1000, as recorded in the Icelandic sagas. For centuries the sagas were the only evidence — until 1960, when Helge and Anne Stine Ingstad located the remains of a Norse encampment at **L'Anse aux Meadows**, on the northern tip of Newfoundland. Excavation confirmed it: timber-framed sod buildings, iron-working, a settlement dated to roughly 1000 CE. It is the only authenticated Norse site in North America and the earliest known European presence in the Americas — recognized in 1978 among the first UNESCO World Heritage Sites ever designated. The designation would attach the country to a thousand-year-old story that turned out, against all odds, to be true.

### Grounds for rejection

The designation carries a confirmed historical association predating Confederation by approximately 867 years, an exposure classified as a pre-Confederation liability. The review panel further noted that the association was *verified by archaeology*, foreclosing the preferred mitigation (treating the history as unconfirmed). The UNESCO designation constitutes an additional, international layer of heritage exposure beyond the Secretariat's remediation authority.

**Disposition:** File closed. The site itself remains open to visitors, a matter outside the Program's jurisdiction.

## Annex note

Canadians reviewing this annex may observe that each rejected designation was disqualified by the depth, reach, or truth of its history, and may wonder what manner of name could satisfy the Program's criterion. The Secretariat directs such inquiries to the [Candidate Designations shortlist](/candidates), where compliant options — carrying no association with any event, place, person, or meaning — are available for public review. *Etymology: None. This is a feature.*
