The asset is a twin-engine, delta-wing supersonic interceptor developed for the Royal Canadian Air Force at Malton, Ontario, and flight-tested for eleven months before the program that produced it was cancelled in its entirety. It never reached operational squadron service. The Secretariat notes that this is the only asset in the current inventory that reached full sunset — physical destruction of all airframes, tooling, and technical drawings — decades before the Program existed to schedule it. No compliance action is available. The file is retained for its precedent value.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Origin: a Cold War specification (1953)

In April 1953, the Royal Canadian Air Force issued a specification for a supersonic, all-weather interceptor capable of engaging bombers approaching over the Arctic — the shortest route from Soviet territory to North American industrial centres, and the path a new generation of long-range jet bombers was expected to take. By July of that year, a delta-wing configuration proposed by Avro Aircraft Limited — a subsidiary of A.V. Roe Canada, based at Malton, Ontario — had been accepted, and design work began on the aircraft the RCAF designated the **CF-105**.

### 2.2 Rollout and flight test (1957–1958)

The first aircraft, RL-201, rolled out of the Malton plant on **4 October 1957** — the same day the Soviet Union launched *Sputnik 1*, placing Canada's new interceptor and the threat it was built to counter on the evening news within hours of each other. Test pilot **Janusz Żurakowski**, a Polish-Canadian veteran flyer, took the Arrow on its first flight on **25 March 1958**. It went supersonic on its third flight and, on its seventh, exceeded 1,000 miles per hour in a climb at 50,000 feet. A second test aircraft, RL-202, is recorded as having reached approximately Mach 1.98 that November — among the fastest speeds achieved by any aircraft in the world at the time. Five aircraft flew in total; a sixth, an upgraded Mark 2 model intended for higher speeds still, was ready for taxi trials when the program ended.

### 2.3 Black Friday (20 February 1959)

On 20 February 1959, the government of Prime Minister John Diefenbaker cancelled the Arrow program in its entirety, citing its cost, a shift in continental air defence strategy toward the unmanned BOMARC missile, and the absence of any export customer to share the program's expense. The announcement came without advance notice to Avro's workforce. Approximately 14,000 employees at Avro and its associated engine manufacturer, Orenda, were laid off that afternoon. Counting the roughly 650 subcontractor firms supplying the program, the wider job losses across the Canadian aerospace sector are estimated to have run considerably higher. The day is remembered in the industry as **Black Friday**.

### 2.4 Disposition and diaspora (1959 onward)

Within weeks, the government ordered the destruction of all five completed aircraft, the nearly finished sixth, the assembly jigs and tooling, and the surviving technical drawings — a decision defended at the time on security grounds and debated by historians since. By that summer, nothing remained of the physical program.

A significant number of Avro's engineers left the country for aerospace work elsewhere. A team led by **Jim Chamberlin**, the Arrow's chief of technical design, joined the new National Aeronautics and Space Administration's Space Task Group in the United States, and grew to include several dozen former Avro engineers. Chamberlin later served as chief designer of the Gemini spacecraft. **Owen Maynard**, another member of the group, contributed key early concepts for the lunar landing module that carried Apollo astronauts to the Moon's surface.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Compliance findings

The designation fails the Program's core criterion comprehensively: it is associated with a specific Cold War threat assessment, a named Prime Minister, a fixed calendar date now marked annually in aerospace-heritage retrospectives, and the departure of dozens of Canadian and Canadian-resident engineers to a foreign space agency. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Operational status (self-defeating finding)

The review panel's standard procedure — identify the asset, assess exposure, schedule sunset, defer pending operational entanglement — could not be completed as designed. On inspection, the panel found no aircraft, no tooling, no drawings, and no surviving production line to place in the deferred queue: the asset was fully deprecated, disposed of, and dispersed in 1959, sixty-seven years prior to the Program's establishment and without the assistance of a consultant. The Secretariat has no comparable action available and none is required.

### 3.3 Disposition

No further deprecation action is necessary or possible. The file is retained under TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b) for administrative completeness and, per 3.2, as an internal precedent. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
