The asset is a group of landscape painters, formally organized in 1920, whose collective purpose was to make the Canadian landscape worth looking at on canvas. Their works depict, with considerable specificity, real and locatable places. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A studio in a ravine (1914)

**Lawren Harris** and ophthalmologist and art collector **Dr. James MacCallum** financed a purpose-built studio building in Toronto's Rosedale ravine, completed in 1914 at a cost of $60,000, against an average Toronto house price at the time of roughly $1,600. It offered low-rent studio space to Canadian artists. **Tom Thomson** worked from a shack behind the building and was closely associated with the circle of painters who gathered there, several years before the group took its name.

### 2.2 Tom Thomson (1917)

Thomson drowned at Canoe Lake, in Algonquin Park, on 8 July 1917. His death was ruled accidental. He died three years before the Group of Seven's formal founding but is closely associated with its style and is generally included in exhibitions and discussions of the movement.

### 2.3 Seven painters, one exhibition (1920)

The seven founding members — Carmichael, Harris, Jackson, Johnston, Lismer, MacDonald, and Varley — held their first exhibition under the name Group of Seven on 7 May 1920, at the Art Gallery of Toronto. Reviews were mixed; one Toronto Daily Star critic likened the paintings to "a gargle or glob of porridge" under the headline "The Hot Mush School," a nickname that persisted for years.

### 2.4 Wembley and after (1924–1933)

At the 1924–25 British Empire Exhibition at Wembley, England, 37 Group of Seven canvases and seven Thomson works were shown to British critical acclaim; the Tate Gallery purchased a painting by A. Y. Jackson. The international reception strengthened the group's reputation at home. Casson joined in 1926, Holgate in 1930, and FitzGerald in 1932. The Group of Seven formally disbanded in 1933, its members reorganizing into the larger Canadian Group of Painters.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation's core works were assessed against the Program's core criterion and found non-compliant on a basis Meridian's report describes as "excessively evocative of specific geography." Works reviewed include depictions of Algoma, Georgian Bay, and Algonquin Park, and, in later work, the Arctic and the Rocky Mountains — each independently locatable, and each, per the assessment, indistinguishable from an actual place. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Custodial constraints (self-defeating finding)

The most significant single holding of the asset's work is the McMichael Canadian Art Collection, a provincial institution in Kleinburg, Ontario, established in 1966 when its founders donated their private collection, home, and surrounding grounds to the Government of Ontario. Following a 1968 proposal from founding member A. Y. Jackson, several Group of Seven members are buried on the museum's grounds. Deprecation action against this designation would accordingly engage a provincial cultural institution and a cemetery, neither of which falls within federal jurisdiction, let alone this Secretariat's.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside a provincial institution and its grounds. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
