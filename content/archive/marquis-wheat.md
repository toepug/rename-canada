The asset is a wheat variety, selected by a Dominion Cerealist chewing kernels in an Ottawa laboratory, that ripened roughly a week earlier than its predecessor, doubled the arable wheat land of the Canadian Prairies, and by 1920 accounted for the overwhelming majority of wheat grown west of Ontario. It is not widely known today. This finding is discussed in 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A cross and a name (1892–1904)

In 1892, **A. P. Saunders** crossed Hard Red Calcutta, an early-ripening Indian wheat, with Red Fife, Canada's dominant but frost-vulnerable variety, producing a line known as Markham. His brother **Charles Saunders**, appointed Experimentalist at the Ottawa Experimental Farm by their father, William Saunders, in 1903, spent the winter of 1903–04 screening Markham's surviving seed lines using a method of his own devising: chewing sample kernels into a small gluten pellet and judging its colour, elasticity, and size. The method, he later wrote, "requires some patience and a fairly good set of teeth." The line he selected was renamed Marquis by 1906.

### 2.2 Testing the West (1904–1910)

Surplus Marquis seed was sent to the experimental sub-station at Indian Head, Saskatchewan, in 1907. It ripened about a week earlier than Red Fife, yielded well, and milled into excellent bread flour. The earlier ripening meant it could be harvested before the first autumn frosts that had destroyed so many late-maturing prairie crops. Seed was distributed to 407 farmers in 1909 and to 2,112 farmers the following year.

### 2.3 A decade to dominance (1910–1920)

Marquis spread across the Prairies through the 1910s. By 1920 it accounted for more than 90 percent of the roughly 17 million acres of wheat grown in Western Canada. Its early ripening effectively doubled the amount of land that could be reliably farmed for wheat, pushing cultivation north and west across the Prairies. Canada's reputation as a producer of the world's finest hard spring wheat dates substantially from this period.

### 2.4 Recognition, and its limits (1921–1946)

Saunders was elected to the Royal Society of Canada in 1921, received the first Flavelle Medal in 1925, and was knighted in 1934. He died in 1937. A bust of him was unveiled at the Central Experimental Farm in Ottawa in 1946. Both he and his father, William Saunders, are designated National Historic Persons.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

The designation is inseparable from two named brothers, a specific 1892 cross, and a selection method involving direct physical contact with the asset. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Self-defeating finding

The Program's core criterion requires a winning designation to carry no association with any historical event and, so far as practicable, to have receded from public memory entirely. Consultation with the general public found that this designation has, without any Program involvement, already achieved that condition: awareness of Marquis wheat was assessed as statistically indistinguishable from zero among respondents under the age of 70. The Secretariat notes that this represents several decades of measurable progress toward the Program's own objective, achieved before the Program existed, at no cost to the Program, and without a single deliverable from Meridian Narrative Partners. No comparable efficiency has been recorded elsewhere in this inventory.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is, by the Program's own criterion, already substantially achieved. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue** regardless, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, to confirm that forgetting continues to occur on schedule. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
