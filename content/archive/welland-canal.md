The asset is a 43.4-kilometre ship canal connecting Lake Ontario to Lake Erie, permitting vessels to bypass Niagara Falls by climbing the Niagara Escarpment — a rise of approximately 99.5 metres, roughly the height of a thirty-storey building, ascended by ships the length of two football fields. The Secretariat notes that the asset has been demolished and rebuilt in its entirety three times, and on every occasion the replacement was given the same name. This pattern of designation persistence has been referred to Phase 0 scoping.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 Origin: the problem of the Falls (1820s)

By the 1820s, the geography of the Great Lakes presented Upper Canada with a commercial emergency. The Erie Canal, opened across New York State in 1825, threatened to draw the trade of the entire continental interior down to Manhattan. Between Lakes Erie and Ontario stood Niagara Falls — magnificent, and completely impassable to shipping.

The answer came substantially from one man: **William Hamilton Merritt**, a St. Catharines mill owner who wanted, initially, a reliable water supply for his mills, and who enlarged that want into a scheme to carry ships over the Escarpment. Merritt raised money, lobbied legislatures, and pushed the project through years of skepticism. On 30 November 1829, two schooners made the first full passage of the First Welland Canal — a channel of wooden locks, forty of them, hauling vessels up the ridge lock by lock. It worked. The trade route through Canada was open.

### 2.2 Four canals, one name (1829–1932)

The canal has never stopped being rebuilt, because ships have never stopped growing:

- **First Canal (1829)** — forty wooden locks; the proof that it could be done at all
- **Second Canal (1840s)** — the wooden locks replaced in cut stone, the count reduced to twenty-seven
- **Third Canal (1887)** — larger stone locks for the steamship era
- **Fourth Canal (opened 1932)** — the present canal: begun in 1913, halted by the First World War, completed as one of the great engineering works of its era. Eight locks now do the work forty once did, each lift approximately 14.2 metres. At Thorold, Locks 4, 5, and 6 are **twinned flight locks** — paired staircases of water cut side by side into the Escarpment so that traffic can climb and descend simultaneously, a structure with few rivals anywhere in the world.

In 1973 the channel was rerouted around the city of Welland, and since 1959 the canal has served as a critical link of the St. Lawrence Seaway, admitting ocean-going vessels to the heart of the continent.

### 2.3 The cost

The Fourth Canal was built substantially by hand, by men working in wet excavations with the tools of the 1910s and 1920s. **One hundred and thirty-seven workers died building it.** For decades they went largely unrecorded; in 2017, a memorial naming each of them was unveiled beside Lock 3. Their names are part of this asset's record.

### 2.4 Present service

The canal remains in full operation. Each year roughly three thousand vessel transits carry tens of millions of tonnes of cargo — grain from the Prairies moving east, iron ore moving to the mills, the working freight of the continent — over a waterfall, by staircase, as they have for 197 years.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming exposure

The designation exhibits layered exposure. The canal is named for the Welland River, which is named for the River Welland in Lincolnshire, England — a chain of reference crossing an ocean and several centuries, terminating in a river that has no involvement in the asset whatsoever. The designation additionally commemorates, by association, Merritt, the schooners of 1829, four generations of engineering, and the 137 (see 2.3). Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Operational entanglement

The asset presents the Program's most difficult deprecation profile: it is historical and *load-bearing at the same time*. The heritage exposure cannot be sunset without addressing the canal, and the canal is currently in use by ships. The Secretariat has determined that interrupting the movement of national grain shipments in order to complete a naming compliance action would generate a historical event, compounding the exposure the action was intended to resolve.

### 3.3 Sunset rationale and disposition

The designation fails the Program's core criterion (no association with any historical event whatsoever) on all measured axes. Sunset is accordingly scheduled. In view of 3.2, however, the asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, while remaining in service and retaining its name. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
