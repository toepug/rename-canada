The asset is a dish of french fries, cheese curds, and gravy, claimed by two towns in Quebec's Centre-du-Québec region as their own invention. Neither claim has been resolved in the nearly seventy years since. This finding, and the Program's own attempt to assign the asset a single file number, are discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 A word, in a bag (1957)

**Fernand Lachance**, owner of Le Café Idéal (later renamed Le Lutin qui rit) in Warwick, Quebec, is said to have exclaimed *"ça va faire une maudite poutine!"* — roughly, "that's going to make a damn mess" — in 1957, when a regular customer asked him to add cheese curds to a takeout bag of fries. "Poutine" was already Quebec slang for a mess or a hodgepodge. In 1963, Lachance began serving the combination on a plate instead of in a bag; when customers complained the fries cooled too quickly, he added gravy to keep them warm.

### 2.2 A dish, on a menu (1958–1964)

In Drummondville, roughly 20 kilometres away, **Jean-Paul Roy** began serving fries with gravy at his drive-in restaurant in 1958; customers began independently ordering a side of cheese curds to add to it. In 1964, Roy took over a drive-in called Orange Jucep, renamed it Le Roy Jucep, and formally added the combined dish to the menu, listed as "fromage-patate-sauce." Le Roy Jucep later trademarked the phrase "Inventor of Poutine" in 1998, and secured a further federal trademark registration referencing "Poutine" in 2007.

### 2.3 A dispute, unresolved (1957–present)

The two claims credit different things: Warwick's, for coining the name and first combining fries and curds; Drummondville's, for being first to serve all three components — fries, curds, and gravy — together as a standing menu item. Food researcher Sylvain Charlebois has publicly split the difference, crediting Warwick as poutine's birthplace and Roy as its inventor. Neither municipality has conceded the question, and no third-party body has ruled on it.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and provenance exposure

This file could not be assigned a year of origin without adjudicating a dispute this Secretariat has no authority to settle. 1957 was selected for administrative purposes only, on the basis that it is the earlier of the two competing claims, and should not be read as a finding in Warwick's favour. Drummondville has been so advised. The designation is additionally inseparable from two named restaurateurs, two municipalities, and a registered federal trademark. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe, independently, in both jurisdictions.

### 3.2 Trademark constraints (self-defeating finding)

One of the two disputing parties holds a federal trademark registration connected to the designation's name, obtained in 2007. Any Program communication using the word "poutine" without qualification risks being read, by the other claimant, as institutional endorsement of a registered mark it does not hold. Legal counsel has recommended the word not be used at all in this file. It has been used ten times.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, in whichever municipality is prepared to host the review first. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
