The asset is a 7,821-kilometre road connecting St. John's, Newfoundland, to Victoria, British Columbia, ceremonially declared complete nine years before it actually was. This finding is discussed in 3.1.

## 2.0 Historical record

*Documented per TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b).*

### 2.1 An act and a decade (1949–1962)

The *Trans-Canada Highway Act*, passed in 1949, authorized construction on a fifty-fifty cost-sharing basis between the federal government and the provinces. Construction began in 1950 across terrain that included muskeg, boreal forest, and three mountain ranges. The last major gap in the route ran through Rogers Pass, in British Columbia's Selkirk Mountains.

### 2.2 Rogers Pass (1881–1962)

Major A. B. Rogers surveyed the pass in 1881 for the Canadian Pacific Railway. The rail line through it proved dangerous: over 200 people were killed by avalanches there in the following decades, including 58 workers killed by a single slide on 4 March 1910, the worst avalanche disaster in Canadian history. The CPR abandoned the pass for rail traffic afterward, boring the five-mile Connaught Tunnel under Mount Macdonald instead, completed in 1916. Rogers Pass sat largely unused until highway engineers selected it, decades later, as the final link of the Trans-Canada route through the Selkirks.

### 2.3 Two openings (1962)

Prime Minister John Diefenbaker presided over the ceremonial opening of the Trans-Canada Highway at Rogers Pass on 3 September 1962, attended by representatives of all ten provinces. British Columbia's own premier was not among them: W. A. C. Bennett, in a dispute with the federal government over funding, declined to attend and instead held a separate, competing "official" opening at the identical location five weeks earlier, on 30 July 1962.

### 2.4 Nine more years (1962–1971)

Substantial sections of the highway remained unfinished or unpaved after the 1962 ceremony. Construction continued for nearly a decade further. The final gap, on Highway 16 in the upper Fraser Valley east of Prince George, was completed in 1971, at which point the highway network met the standard set out in the 1949 Act.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Naming and completion exposure

The designation was declared complete on 3 September 1962, in a ceremony attended by the Prime Minister and representatives of all ten provinces, nine years before construction was, in fact, complete. The Secretariat notes this as the closest documented precedent for the Program's own phased delivery model: a functioning completion ceremony, held in advance of the underlying work, generating national coverage without requiring the asset to be finished. No comparable efficiency has been achieved since. Fact Density was assessed by Meridian as severe.

### 3.2 Jurisdictional fragmentation (self-defeating finding)

The asset is not owned, constructed, or maintained by any single body. Each province and territory it passes through retains full jurisdiction over its own section, funds maintenance independently, and — in a detail the Secretariat finds difficult to improve upon — designs its own physical shape for the highway shield marking the route, such that no two provinces display an identical sign for the same national highway. There is, in the relevant administrative sense, no single asset here for the Secretariat to engage.

### 3.3 Disposition

Sunset is warranted on the merits and cannot presently be executed. The asset has been placed in the **deferred queue**, where it will be reviewed quarterly, indefinitely, alongside the thirteen separate jurisdictions that would each need to agree first. The Secretariat is satisfied that this constitutes progress.
