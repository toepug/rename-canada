The designation "Canada" is the longest-serving national identifier asset in the Secretariat's inventory and the first entry logged in the Legacy Deprecation Archive. Its service record is, by any measure, distinguished. This is precisely the problem. See 3.2.

## 2.0 Historical record

*The Secretariat is required under TB Reg. 2024-117, s. 12(b) to document the full provenance of any designation prior to sunset. The following record satisfies that requirement.*

### 2.1 Origin: kanata (1535)

The name derives from **kanata**, a word in the language of the St. Lawrence Iroquoians meaning "village" or "settlement."

In the summer of 1535, on his second voyage up the river he would call the Rivière du Canada — now the St. Lawrence — the French navigator Jacques Cartier reached the village of **Stadacona**, near the site of present-day Quebec City. Two young Iroquoians, Domagaya and Taignoagny, sons of the chief Donnacona, had travelled to France with Cartier after his first voyage and returned with him as guides and interpreters. From them, Cartier recorded the word *kanata* — the ordinary word for the village and the settled land around it — and began applying "Canada" to the whole region under Donnacona's influence.

The word spread the way words do when written down by people drawing maps: outward, past every boundary its original speakers intended. Within a generation "Canada" appeared on European world maps, attached to an area growing larger with each printing. By the time Samuel de Champlain arrived in 1603, the name had outlived the people who coined it — the St. Lawrence Iroquoians had vanished from the valley by the 1580s, dispersed by causes still debated: war, disease, migration. Their language survives principally in the word lists Cartier wrote down, and in the name of the country.

The Secretariat notes for the record that the national identifier scheduled for sunset is, at its root, a 491-year-old borrowed word meaning *the village*.

### 2.2 Cartographic propagation (1547–1763)

"Canada" appears on European maps from approximately 1547 onward, initially denoting the St. Lawrence valley. Under the French regime, "Canada" named the most populous district of New France, and its inhabitants — *les Canadiens* — carried the demonym long before any political entity matched it. Following the Seven Years' War and the 1763 Treaty of Paris, British administrators inherited a name already three generations deep in local usage and, in a rare display of restraint the Secretariat does not endorse as precedent, kept it.

### 2.3 Political consolidation (1791–1867)

- **1791** — The Constitutional Act divides the old Province of Quebec into **Upper Canada** and **Lower Canada**, giving the name its first formal statutory footing in English law.
- **1841** — The Act of Union merges the two into the **Province of Canada**.
- **1867** — The British North America Act creates the **Dominion of Canada**. Alternative national designations were proposed and debated during Confederation — among them Borealia, Tuponia, Hochelaga, Cabotia, Efisga, and Laurentia — in an uncontrolled naming exercise conducted without consultants, resonance testing, or a single deliverable. The exercise concluded in months and its output has remained in service for 159 years. The Secretariat has classified this episode separately as a methodological hazard. See the [Precedent File: The 1867 Naming Exercise](/archive/1867-naming-exercise).

### 2.4 Competing etymologies (dismissed)

A persistent folk legend attributes the name to Spanish or Portuguese explorers who, finding no gold, marked the territory *"acá nada"* — "nothing here" — from which "Canada" supposedly derives. This etymology is apocryphal and rejected by scholars.

The Secretariat wishes to note that it reviewed this etymology with genuine interest, as a national identifier documented to mean "nothing here" would have satisfied the Program's core criterion (no meaningful historical association) and might have permitted retention of the existing designation at considerable savings. Regrettably, the true etymology (2.1) is well attested. The name means something. The file was closed.

## 3.0 Deprecation assessment

### 3.1 Heritage exposure findings

Preliminary Phase 0 scoping (see Meridian Narrative Partners, *Legacy Risk Register: National Heritage Exposure Audit*, 2025) identified the following exposure associated with the legacy designation:

| Exposure category | Instances (est.) |
|---|---|
| Historical events referencing the designation | 100% of national historical events |
| Treaties, statutes, and instruments bearing the designation | 68,000+ |
| Songs, poems, and literary works | Not fully enumerable |
| Persons emotionally attached to the designation | Under survey; early figures described by Meridian as "concerning" |

### 3.2 Sunset rationale

Under the Program's core evaluation criterion, a compliant national identifier must carry **no association with any historical event whatsoever**, as all recorded history has been assessed as containing grievance, complexity, or meaning.

The legacy designation fails this criterion comprehensively. It is associated with, among other liabilities: a 16th-century Indigenous word; the entire documentary record of two colonial empires; Confederation; both World Wars; the Charter; and every event, achievement, tragedy, and Tim Hortons opening of the national past. Its etymology is not merely historical but *irreducibly* historical — the word cannot be spoken without reference to a specific river, a specific village, and specific people.

The Secretariat has determined that this is a liability.

### 3.3 Compliance notes

⚠ **Official languages requirements:** The legacy designation is identical in English and French, a bilingual efficiency no shortlisted candidate has yet matched. This finding has been referred to Phase 0 scoping for additional scoping.

A proposal to resolve the etymology exposure by adopting the original form of the word was received through public intake and reviewed. See [Rejected Designations: Kanata](/archive/rejected-designations#kanata) — rejected as *excessively rooted in heritage*.

## 4.0 Succession planning

Succession options are catalogued under the [Candidate Designations shortlist](/candidates). As of the Q2 2026 Interim Tabulation, the Secretariat continues to have confidence in the Kanada option.

The legacy designation will remain in service throughout Phase 0 and all subsequent phases until a successor designation completes dignity compliance review, stakeholder revalidation, and signage procurement (est. Phase 5, 2038–2041). Canadians are advised that no action is required at this time, or, per current projections, for the foreseeable future.
