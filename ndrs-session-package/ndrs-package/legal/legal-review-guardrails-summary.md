## 1. Project description

renamecanada.ca is a satirical website presented as a fictional federal pilot program — the "National Designation Renewal Secretariat" (NDRS) — earnestly campaigning to rename Canada. The satire targets bureaucratic renaming exercises, consultant-driven rebranding, and performative gestures substituting for engagement with history. Beneath the deadpan surface, the site's "Legacy Deprecation Archive" preserves genuine, researched Canadian history framed as content scheduled for deletion.

The satirical mode is a Swiftian "modest proposal": full commitment to the absurd position, no winking on main pages. Reference model for register and legal posture: The Beaverton — unmistakably governmental in flavour, unmistakably fictional on inspection.

The site is a non-commercial personal project. There is no advertising and it does not accept donations — the in-character "Support the Program" page is a dead-end (Phase 0 scoping, no payment mechanism). A pointer to two real, unaffiliated heritage charities (National Trust for Canada; Canada's History Society) appears only on the out-of-character disclaimer page, not as a donate destination.

## 2. Identity marks — what the site does not use

The design was built from the outset around official-marks and impersonation risk:

- No Government of Canada wordmark, coat of arms, Canadian flag glyphs, or any mark protected under s. 9 of the Trade-marks Act
- No Parliament silhouette (the real Canada.ca footer watermark is replaced with a silhouette of the invented crest)
- No real ministry, department, or agency names anywhere; the invented ministry name appears in every position a real one would occupy
- No gc.ca-style URL structures; the domain is renamecanada.ca, registered personally
- The crest is an original invented design intended to rhyme with heraldic government marks without copying any real mark [crest files included in package]

## 3. What the site deliberately borrows

The pastiche reads through structure rather than marks, at roughly 85% fidelity to Canada.ca conventions:

- Page anatomy: bilingual header signature, breadcrumbs, red H1 accent bar, "Date modified" lines, two-tier footer, feedback box
- Typography: Lato and Noto Sans (both openly licensed Google Fonts, and the pairing used by Canada.ca)
- Colour palette matching Canada.ca conventions (e.g., #AF3C43 accent red, #26374A navy)

The design intent is that structural resemblance establishes the genre while the swapped identity marks, invented ministry, and absurd mandate carry the fictional signal.

## 4. Fiction discoverability

Layered so the fiction is discoverable on inspection by any visitor:

1. The mandate itself (renaming Canada because history "contains grievance") is absurd on its face
2. The ministry name is invented and returns no real-government results
3. Every page footer links to a satire disclaimer; the /disclaimer page is written in plain, out-of-character language [text included in package]
4. In-content tells throughout (e.g., a candidate name of "Nameless McNameface" leading the official vote)

## 5. Content guardrails (applied to all published material)

- Satirical targets are exclusively institutional: bureaucratic process, consultant-industry practices, costs of symbolic change, stakeholder-engagement theatre. Litmus test applied to every joke: if the target is a choice (a policy, budget, strategy), it is eligible; if the target is who someone is, it is cut
- No jokes aimed at ethnic groups, immigrant communities, or any identity group; no stereotypes
- Real names and histories — including Indigenous place names discussed in the "Rejected Designations" section — are treated with full respect and genuine research; the fictional ministry's rejection rationales are the only joke
- No real brands are ever named. A candidate satirizing naming-rights culture ("Canada presented by [SPONSOR PENDING]") refers only to sector categories, never companies
- Invented "international" candidate names are always fabricated words, never real words borrowed from actual languages or cultures
- All user-submitted content passes through private manual curation before anything derived from it is published; nothing publishes automatically

## 6. Fictional entities

**Meridian Narrative Partners** — the fictional consulting firm to which the site's fake reports are attributed. Wholly invented: no real principals, employees, offices, or clients are named; all budget figures and engagement details are invented. Preliminary lay searches found no company with this exact name, but adjacent names exist (a "Meridian Narrative, LLC" in Wyoming; several unrelated "Meridian"-prefixed consultancies; Meridian Credit Union in Ontario).

**National Designation Renewal Secretariat** — the fictional ministry. Name invented; no real body with this name found in lay searches.

## 7. Questions for counsel

1. Official-marks proximity: does the crest and overall design maintain adequate distance from s. 9 marks and from anything that could constitute government impersonation?
2. Disclaimer adequacy: is the disclaimer's wording and placement (footer link on every page) sufficient, or should it be more prominent?
3. Defamation exposure: is the fictional-entity framing of the consultant reports adequate? Does "Meridian Narrative Partners" require more distance from similarly named real firms?
4. Any concerns with the domain name renamecanada.ca itself?

Note: the site takes no donations and names no destination charity, so the earlier question about donate-destination consent no longer applies. The disclaimer does link out informationally to two real heritage charities as reading suggestions (no affiliation claimed, no funds change hands); flag this only if it seems worth raising unprompted.
